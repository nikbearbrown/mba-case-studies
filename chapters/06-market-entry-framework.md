# Chapter 6 — Market Entry Framework

Every market entry decision is, at its core, a bet.

The company is wagering capital, management attention, organizational energy, and often a piece of its existing business against the proposition that a new market will reward the investment. Sometimes the bet is modest — a small product line extension into an adjacent geography, low stakes, reversible if it doesn't work. Sometimes it is enormous — a full greenfield build in a foreign country, years of losses before profitability, a commitment so large that failure would damage the parent company seriously.

What makes market entry analytically interesting is that the bet has two completely separate components that candidates routinely collapse into one. The first is whether the market is worth entering at all — whether the economics and competitive dynamics make it attractive enough to justify the investment. The second is how to enter — which specific mode of entry gives the company the best chance of success given its capabilities and the market's characteristics.

Collapsing these two questions produces bad analysis. A company can correctly identify that a market is attractive and then destroy value by entering it the wrong way. Conversely, a company can develop a brilliant entry strategy for a market that isn't actually worth entering. The two questions require different analytical tools and different kinds of evidence, and treating them as one question means you'll answer neither well.

This chapter is about how to answer both.

---

## Before the Framework: The Questions That Set Up Everything

There is a discipline in market entry analysis that separates strong candidates from weak ones, and it happens before any framework gets applied. It is the discipline of understanding what the company actually wants and why.

"Enter a new market" is not a strategy. It is an intention. Behind that intention is a motivation, and the motivation matters enormously because it determines what success looks like and therefore what analysis is required.

A company might be considering market entry because its domestic market has saturated and growth requires new geography. That's a growth imperative. Or because a competitor has moved into the target market and the company needs a defensive presence. That's a competitive response. Or because the company has a product that is uniquely suited to conditions in the target market — regulatory environment, demographic profile, unmet need. That's an opportunity exploitation. Or because the company's investors are demanding a geographic diversification story. That's a financial narrative requirement that may or may not correspond to genuine strategic logic.

Each motivation implies a different threshold for what counts as a sufficient return on investment. A defensive market entry might be worth executing even at breakeven, if the cost of ceding the market to a competitor is high enough. An opportunistic entry into an attractive market might require a higher return threshold because the company has no particular urgency. A growth-imperative entry needs to actually deliver growth, which means market size and growth rate matter more than margins, at least in the short term.

So before you touch a framework, ask: what is driving the decision to consider this? And what does success look like from the client's perspective? These two questions will orient every subsequent piece of analysis.

The second piece of setup is understanding the company itself — its product portfolio, its operational model, its experience with previous market entries, and its existing capabilities along the value chain. A company that has successfully entered ten new markets has organizational muscle memory that a company making its first international move doesn't have. A company that makes premium products has a different challenge entering a price-sensitive emerging market than a company that has always competed on cost. A company that manages its own distribution in its home market may find that capability transfers to the new market, or may find that local distribution networks are entirely relationship-dependent in ways that make organic entry nearly impossible.

These are not details to be addressed after the strategic decision. They are inputs to the strategic decision.

## Is the Market Worth Entering?

With the setup done, the first major analytical question is attractiveness: should the company enter this market at all?

The framework that structures this analysis has four pillars, and each pillar asks a distinct question about the market.

**The Customer pillar** asks who is actually in this market and what they want. This means segmenting the potential customer base — not just demographics, but behavioral segments defined by what they're trying to accomplish and what they currently use to accomplish it. It means estimating market size and growth rate: how many potential customers are there, what would they spend, and is that number growing or contracting? It means identifying the target group most likely to adopt the company's offering and understanding what share of the total market that group represents. And it means being honest about market share: given the competitive environment, what fraction of addressable demand could the company realistically capture, and over what timeframe?

Market sizing is often the most quantitatively intensive part of this analysis, and it deserves care. The method matters. A top-down approach starts from macro data — total population, income distribution, category spend as a percentage of income — and works down to an addressable segment. A bottom-up approach starts from the customer unit and builds up — if we can reach X distribution points and each point generates Y customers at Z revenue each, we get to a total. Neither method is inherently superior; the right choice depends on what data is available and which direction of estimation has fewer uncertain assumptions. In case interviews, doing both and triangulating between them is a powerful demonstration of analytical rigor.

**The Company pillar** asks whether this company specifically is equipped to win in this market. This is the mirror image of the customer question — where the customer question asks about demand, the company question asks about capability supply.

The relevant analysis here runs through the value chain. Can the company procure inputs cost-effectively for this market? Can it manufacture or develop products that meet this market's specific requirements? Can it build or access distribution that reaches target customers? Does it have the brand presence or marketing capability to acquire customers in an unfamiliar environment? Each stage of the value chain may need to be built, acquired, or partnered into existence, and the cost and feasibility of doing so is a major input to the entry decision.

The financial analysis sits here too. The core calculation is the same regardless of market: projected revenue minus projected costs equals projected profit, and projected profit minus investment required to enter equals projected return. The question is whether the return exceeds the company's cost of capital by enough to justify the risk. In practice, this calculation requires assumptions about market share capture rate, pricing levels, and cost structure — assumptions that need to be stress-tested. What does the economics look like if market share capture takes twice as long as projected? What if local cost inputs are 20% higher than estimated? The range of outcomes, not just the base case, is what tells you whether the market is really worth entering.

**The Competition pillar** asks who is already in the market and what it would cost to displace or coexist with them.

The relevant dimensions here are number of competitors and market share concentration, the competitive advantages of existing players, barriers to entry and exit, and the regulatory environment. A market with three entrenched competitors who have deep local relationships and regulatory protections is categorically different from a fragmented market where no player has dominant share. The first is difficult to enter and the incumbent response to new entry will be aggressive. The second may be genuinely open.

The barrier-to-entry analysis deserves particular attention because barriers are asymmetric: some barriers make entry hard for everyone, while others specifically disadvantage companies without local presence, local relationships, or local regulatory approval. A foreign company entering a market where government contracts dominate faces barriers that a local company doesn't. A company entering a market where the dominant competitor owns exclusive distribution relationships faces barriers that product quality or price advantage alone cannot overcome.

SWOT analysis belongs here as a synthesis tool — not as a template to fill in exhaustively, but as a structured way of making explicit what the company brings to the market that incumbents don't have, and what incumbents have that the entering company will need to build or acquire.

**The Product pillar** asks whether there is actually a gap between what customers want and what they currently have. This question is deceptively important and frequently skipped in case analysis.

A market can look attractive on every other dimension — large, growing, underpenetrated, lightly defended — and still be a poor entry target if customers are broadly satisfied with existing offerings. Switching costs are real. The inertia of established habits is real. A company entering a market with a product that is incrementally better than the incumbent offering faces a harder adoption challenge than a company entering with a product that addresses a genuine unmet need — something customers want and cannot currently get.

The gap analysis requires understanding not just what customers currently use, but what they currently complain about, what workarounds they've developed, what they would use if it existed. That kind of customer insight is sometimes available through secondary research; it often requires primary investigation that the case won't provide, which means the answer you develop will be a hypothesis to be validated rather than a conclusion.

## How to Enter: The Mode Decision

If the attractiveness analysis concludes that the market is worth entering, the second question is how. This is where entry mode strategy lives, and the four main options — acquisition, greenfield build, joint venture, and outsourcing/licensing — have fundamentally different risk profiles, cost structures, and capability requirements.

**Acquisition** — sometimes called brownfield investment — means buying an existing player in the target market. The advantage is speed: you acquire market presence, customer relationships, local brand equity, distribution networks, and operational capability in a single transaction. The disadvantages are cost (acquisitions require paying a premium over book value), integration risk (merging two organizations is genuinely hard and often destroys the value you paid for), and the risk that what you're acquiring is not actually what it appeared to be during due diligence.

Acquisition makes most sense when time is a critical constraint, when the competitive dynamics of the market are moving fast enough that organic entry would be too slow, or when the capabilities required to compete in the market would take too long to build. It also makes sense when an attractive acquisition target exists — when there is a specific company whose assets, relationships, or capabilities are worth more to the entering company than their standalone value.

The key question to ask about an acquisition is whether the value being acquired is truly transferable. Brand equity in a local market is often highly dependent on the founding team or existing management — if they leave post-acquisition, the brand equity may leave with them. Customer relationships may be personal rather than institutional. Regulatory licenses may not transfer. What you see in the target may not be what you get after the deal closes.

**Greenfield** means building from scratch — establishing new operations, hiring local talent, building distribution relationships, investing in brand awareness, developing local supply chains. The advantage is control: you build exactly what you want, with the culture and processes and capabilities you intend. The disadvantages are time and cost. A greenfield build typically takes years to reach operating efficiency and even longer to reach profitability. During that time, the company is absorbing losses and management attention, and the market may move in ways that make the original investment thesis less compelling.

Greenfield makes sense when the company has sufficient financial staying power to absorb the investment period, when there is no suitable acquisition target, when control over the operation is strategically important, and when the time horizon is long enough that the build-up period is acceptable. It also makes sense when the company's competitive advantage is deeply embedded in its processes and culture — things that would be diluted or corrupted by acquiring a local incumbent with different ways of operating.

**Joint venture** means partnering with a local entity to share the investment, risk, and (eventually) returns from market entry. The local partner contributes market knowledge, relationships, regulatory standing, and distribution access. The entering company contributes capital, product, technology, or brand. The advantage over greenfield is that the local partner's knowledge and relationships dramatically reduce the learning curve. The advantage over acquisition is that you're not paying full price for capabilities you're only partially leveraging.

The disadvantages of joint ventures are well-documented and serious. Governance is inherently complex when two organizations with different cultures, different incentive structures, and potentially different long-term objectives share control of an entity. What happens when the joint venture needs more capital and the partners disagree about how much to invest? What happens when one partner wants to expand into adjacent markets and the other doesn't? What happens if the relationship between the parent companies deteriorates for reasons unrelated to the venture? Joint ventures have a high failure rate relative to their strategic promise, and the most common reason is not commercial failure but governance failure.

Joint ventures make the most sense in markets where local knowledge and relationships are the critical success factor and where regulatory requirements mandate local partnership (many countries require majority local ownership in specific sectors). They require significant investment in governance design upfront — clear decision rights, explicit provisions for capital calls, agreed exit mechanisms — to have a reasonable chance of working.

**Outsourcing and licensing** represent the lightest-touch entry options. The company licenses its technology, brand, or product design to a local operator, who manufactures and distributes under some form of contractual arrangement. The entering company receives royalties or license fees without bearing the capital cost of building local operations.

The appeal is obvious: low investment, low risk, capital-efficient exposure to the market. The problem is equally obvious: low control. The licensee is running the business, and their incentives may not align perfectly with the licensor's. If the licensee cuts costs in ways that damage product quality, the entering company's brand in that market suffers. If the licensee builds distribution relationships that give them bargaining power in renegotiating the license terms, the entering company's economics deteriorate. Licensing is often a market test — a low-commitment way of establishing presence before deciding whether to invest in a more substantial entry mode — rather than a permanent strategic position.

## The Entry Decision as a System

The entry mode decision cannot be made independently of the attractiveness analysis, and neither can be made independently of the company's strategic intent and capability profile. These three components — intent, attractiveness, and entry mode — form a system, and the analysis has to hold together across all three.

Consider: a company with a highly differentiated product and strong manufacturing capabilities but no experience with international logistics or local market knowledge. The attractiveness analysis might show that a particular market is genuinely attractive. But the greenfield option would require building distribution and local knowledge from scratch, which takes too long. The acquisition option requires finding a target whose distribution assets are strong enough to justify the premium. The joint venture option might be best — it provides the local knowledge the company lacks — but only if the company can find a partner whose capabilities genuinely complement its own rather than overlap with them.

The analysis isn't complete until these pieces connect. The conclusion isn't just "this market is attractive" or "use a joint venture." It is: "This market is attractive because of X and Y, the company can compete because of Z, the preferred entry mode is joint venture because the company lacks local distribution and the regulatory environment requires local partnership, and the financial return under that mode at realistic market share assumptions exceeds the cost of capital by a sufficient margin to justify the risk."

That's a recommendation. Everything before it is analysis. The discipline of market entry analysis is holding all the components simultaneously until the system produces a coherent answer.

## What Market Entry Analysis Is Actually Testing

In a case interview context, market entry cases are testing something specific: whether you can think simultaneously about external attractiveness and internal capability, and whether you understand that the right answer is not always yes.

Many candidates, especially those who have been trained on growth strategy, approach market entry cases with an implicit bias toward recommending entry. The market looks attractive, the company has some relevant capabilities, there's a reasonable entry mode — let's go. This is almost always wrong, or at least incomplete. The interesting analytical work is in the cases where the market looks attractive but the company's capability gaps make entry inadvisable, or where the market is moderately attractive but the company has such a strong competitive position that even a moderate market would generate excellent returns.

The other thing being tested is quantitative judgment. Market entry cases almost always have a financial component — you need to estimate market size, project market share, model the economics of a specific entry mode, calculate a breakeven point. These calculations require assumptions, and the assumptions need to be internally consistent and defensible. A market share projection of 25% in year one is almost never defensible for a new entrant in a competitive market, regardless of how differentiated the product is. A positive NPV calculated using optimistic assumptions on every variable is not evidence that the market is worth entering; it's evidence that optimistic assumptions produce optimistic results.

The market entry framework is ultimately a structure for disciplined optimism — for being enthusiastic about opportunities while being honest about what they require and what might go wrong. Companies that enter markets for good reasons, with clear eyes about the challenges, executing the right entry mode for their specific capabilities, tend to succeed. Companies that enter markets because the market looks big and the framework said yes tend to fail in predictable ways.

The analysis should prepare you to tell the difference.

## A note about AI

Market entry questions are where the model is most fluently misleading. The framework is well-known, so the model produces confident output. The output rarely engages with the entry-specific friction that makes real cases hard.

Where the model genuinely helps: producing the standard market-entry checklist (size, growth, competition, channels, regulation, capabilities) faster than you can recall it. Useful as a memory aid.

Where the model does damage: writing the answer to a specific market entry case. The specifics — your client's actual capabilities, the local regulatory environment, the channel partners that actually exist — are not in the model's training in any actionable way.

The rule: framework from the model, judgment from you.
