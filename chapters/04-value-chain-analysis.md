# Chapter 4 — Value Chain Analysis
*Where the $135 Goes — and Where It Disappears.*

Pick any company that makes a physical product — say, a running shoe. Raw materials come in one end: rubber, foam, synthetic fabric, thread. A finished shoe comes out the other. Between those two points, something happens that is worth a great deal more than the sum of its inputs. The rubber and foam and fabric, assembled in the right configuration, by the right process, marketed to the right person through the right channel, becomes something a consumer will pay $150 for. The materials themselves cost perhaps $15. What accounts for the other $135?

That gap — between what inputs cost and what outputs sell for — is where value chain analysis lives. It is the framework for understanding how companies actually create value, where they spend money creating it, and which parts of the process are working well versus which parts are leaking value they should be capturing.

This matters in case interviews because a surprising fraction of all business problems, when traced carefully, turn out to be value chain problems in disguise. A company's margins are declining — where in the chain is value being lost? A competitor is undercutting on price — at which stage of production are they achieving a cost advantage? A new market entry is failing — where in the chain does the company lack the capability to serve this market? These questions cannot be answered without a systematic way of decomposing how the business actually works.

---

Michael Porter introduced the formal concept of the value chain in 1985, but the underlying idea is older and simpler: a business is not a single thing, it is a sequence of activities, and each activity both consumes resources and creates — or destroys — value. The value chain is just a map of those activities, organized in the order they occur.

Porter divided activities into two categories. Primary activities are the ones directly involved in creating, delivering, and supporting the product: inbound logistics, operations, outbound logistics, marketing and sales, and service. Support activities are the ones that enable primary activities to function: procurement, technology development, human resource management, and firm infrastructure. This taxonomy is useful for thinking comprehensively, but in practice you'll use a more granular, industry-specific version — tracing the particular sequence of activities through which this business creates value, not the generic five-category structure.

A reasonable map for a physical goods company runs like this: Product Development and R&D → Sourcing of Materials → Manufacturing → Warehousing and Transportation Logistics → Distribution → Retail and Marketing → After-Sales Service and Support. For a software company, the activities look different — product development and engineering dominate, logistics nearly disappears, and customer success takes the place of after-sales repair. For a professional services firm, the chain transforms almost entirely into people-acquisition and knowledge-application stages. The specific activities are determined by the specific business, not by the framework. The framework is a prompt to think comprehensively, not a straitjacket.

---

The word "chain" is doing real work here, and it's worth pausing on why.

A chain has a specific physical property: it is only as strong as its weakest link. Pull on a chain and it doesn't distribute force evenly — it snaps at the weakest point, regardless of how strong all the other links are. Value chains work the same way. A company can have world-class manufacturing but catastrophic distribution, and the catastrophic distribution will undermine the world-class manufacturing completely. The finished goods will sit in warehouses or arrive damaged or reach the wrong channels. Customers who might have been delighted by the product will never encounter it.

This property has a diagnostic implication. When you're analyzing a cost structure or profitability problem using a value chain, you are looking for the weak link. Not evaluating every stage comprehensively for its own sake — that takes forever and produces too much material to synthesize. You're trying to identify which stage is responsible for the problem, so you can concentrate analytical attention there.

How do you find the weak link? Two methods.

The first is to ask where costs are concentrated relative to what you'd expect. If a company is spending 40% of its cost of goods sold on logistics in an industry where that figure is typically 15%, you've found something worth examining. If manufacturing costs have been flat while distribution costs have risen 30% over two years, the chain is developing a weakness in a specific place.

The second is to ask where performance has deteriorated over time or diverged from competitors. If a company's on-time delivery rates have declined from 94% to 87% while a competitor's have held at 95%, that's a logistics problem. If customer satisfaction scores have dropped specifically on post-purchase support while the product itself receives high marks, that's an after-sales problem. The value chain gives you the vocabulary to name where the deterioration is happening.

The chain metaphor also captures something easily missed: activities are connected, not independent. What happens at one stage constrains and shapes what's possible at the next. Poor sourcing decisions create manufacturing problems downstream. Manufacturing defects create after-sales costs at the end. Over-investment in retail marketing that drives demand the supply chain can't fulfill creates customer experience failures everywhere. When you change something in one part of the chain, you need to trace the implications forward and backward through everything the changed activity touches.

---

In case interviews, value chain analysis is most commonly applied as a cost analysis tool. The question it answers is: where does the money go, and is that distribution of spending appropriate?

This requires distinguishing between two types of costs that behave very differently. Fixed costs don't change with volume — at least not in the short run. Factory rent is fixed whether you make 10,000 units or 100,000. The R&D team's salaries are fixed. When you have high fixed costs, the unit economics of your business are highly sensitive to volume: as volume increases, fixed costs spread over more units and profitability improves dramatically. As volume decreases, those costs concentrate on fewer units and margins compress just as dramatically. Variable costs change with volume. Raw materials, packaging, shipping costs for individual orders — these scale with the business. When variable costs dominate your cost structure, your margins per unit are more stable but your ability to achieve economies of scale is limited.

Understanding which costs in each stage are fixed versus variable tells you how to think about the business's profitability dynamics. A company with high fixed costs in manufacturing benefits enormously from volume — every incremental unit sold above breakeven contributes almost entirely to profit. A company with high variable costs in distribution needs to negotiate better rates or find more efficient channels, not simply sell more units.

The third distinction worth making is between direct and indirect costs. Direct costs are traceable to specific units of production: the leather in a shoe, the labor hours on a specific assembly line, the freight cost for a specific shipment. Indirect costs — overhead, management salaries, shared IT infrastructure — support the business generally but can't be cleanly attributed to any specific product or unit. Direct costs tell you where value creation is actually happening. If direct costs are rising in manufacturing while they're stable elsewhere, the manufacturing process is becoming less efficient. Indirect costs are harder to trace but can be equally important: a company that has allowed overhead to balloon while direct costs stayed controlled has a different kind of problem, one that often requires structural rather than operational solutions.

Let's walk through the chain stage by stage, because each stage has its own cost drivers and its own failure modes.

**Product Development and R&D** is where the value chain begins — often invisibly, because R&D costs are incurred long before any revenue appears. The relevant questions are about investment level and effectiveness: is this company spending enough on R&D relative to competitors to maintain product relevance? Is the pipeline generating products customers actually want? Are intellectual property assets being developed and protected in ways that create durable competitive advantage? The failure modes here tend to be slow and hard to detect: inadequate R&D spending shows up as product obsolescence two or three years later, not immediately.

**Sourcing of Materials** is where costs first become concrete. The key questions are about procurement efficiency: what is the company paying for inputs relative to market prices? Do they have supplier concentration risk — too much dependence on a single supplier who can exert pricing power? Are they managing order quantities intelligently to minimize inventory costs while avoiding stockouts? The best companies at this stage have long-term supplier relationships that create preferential pricing, volume commitments that enable planning, and alternative sourcing options that provide negotiating leverage.

**Manufacturing** is often the most complex stage to analyze because it has the most moving parts. Machinery costs, factory rent, electricity, labor hours, technology, capacity utilization, packaging, and defect rates all interact. The single most important concept here is capacity utilization: a factory running at 60% capacity has a very different unit economics profile than the same factory running at 90%, even if everything else is identical. Fixed costs divide over 50% more units in the second case. When a company's manufacturing margins are deteriorating, the first question is almost always whether volume has declined and the fixed cost burden has intensified as a result.

Defect rates deserve special attention because they have a multiplier effect. A defect caught in manufacturing costs whatever it costs to rework or scrap that unit. A defect that gets through manufacturing into distribution costs more — you now also have logistics costs on a unit that generates no revenue. A defect that reaches the customer costs more still: all the upstream costs, plus the after-sales service cost, plus the harder-to-quantify cost to brand equity and customer loyalty.

**Warehousing and Transportation Logistics** is the stage where physical goods companies most often have hidden cost problems. The costs divide naturally into two: getting goods from manufacturing to warehouse, and from warehouse to customer. The relevant questions are about route optimization, vehicle utilization, storage costs, and the trade-off between inventory levels and service quality. High inventory levels reduce the risk of stockouts but increase holding costs — tied-up capital, storage space, insurance, the risk of obsolescence. Low inventory levels reduce holding costs but increase the risk of missing demand and the cost of expedited shipping when stockouts occur. Finding the right inventory level for each product in each market involves forecasting demand accurately, which most companies do imperfectly.

**Distribution** is where the company's go-to-market model becomes visible. Is the company selling direct to consumers, through retail partners, through wholesale distributors, through online channels, or some combination? Each channel has a different cost structure and a different margin profile. A company that sells through retail partners is sharing margin with those partners; a company that sells direct keeps more margin but bears the full customer acquisition cost. Channel mix decisions have profound effects on overall profitability and are often stickier than companies would like — channel partners develop expectations and contractual protections, and changing channel mix is slow and expensive.

**Retail and Marketing** is where demand gets created and converted into sales. The questions here are about marketing efficiency: what is the cost of acquiring a customer, how does that compare to the lifetime value of that customer, and are different channels generating customers at different cost-to-value ratios? Digital marketing has made these metrics measurable in ways that were impossible before, but the proliferation of channels has also complicated the attribution question. Which channel deserves credit for a purchase that involved three touchpoints?

**After-Sales Service and Support** is the stage most frequently underweighted in strategic analysis, and often where significant value is either created or destroyed. The costs include repairs, spare parts, returns processing, and the service level agreements that many business-to-business companies are contractually committed to. The failure mode is treating after-sales as a pure cost center rather than as a source of customer data, loyalty, and in some industries a substantial revenue stream. Companies that design products for serviceability — that make it easy and cheap to repair and maintain — have fundamentally different after-sales cost structures than companies that don't.

---

In a case interview, value chain analysis is a diagnostic tool. You reach for it when the question is about cost structure, operational efficiency, or the feasibility of a strategic move that requires capability in specific parts of the chain.

The practical sequence is: identify the stage or stages where the problem is likely concentrated, form a hypothesis about what specific cost drivers within that stage are behaving abnormally, and then ask for the data that would confirm or disconfirm the hypothesis.

Here is an important discipline: do not audit the entire chain in sequence. Interviewers find this tedious, and it signals that you're using the framework as a crutch rather than as a tool. The chain is a search space. Your job is to identify the most promising part of that search space given what you know and go there directly, explaining why.

"I'd like to focus on manufacturing and logistics first, because the margin compression over the past two years coincides with the opening of their new distribution center, and I'd expect the costs of ramping a new logistics infrastructure to show up most clearly in those two stages." That is a directed, hypothesis-driven use of the framework. "Let me start with R&D and work my way through all seven stages" is not.

The value chain is also essential for new market entry analysis, where the question is not just about external market attractiveness but about whether the company has the capabilities in each relevant stage to serve the new market. A company considering expansion into Southeast Asia needs to think about whether their existing sourcing relationships include suppliers who can serve that region cost-effectively, whether their manufacturing can accommodate different product specifications, whether their logistics infrastructure extends there or would need to be built, and whether their distribution relationships transfer or need to be rebuilt from scratch. Missing capability in any stage can be a decisive barrier to entry.

---

The value chain works as an analytical tool because it encodes a specific insight about how businesses create value: value creation is not a single act but a sequence of acts, each of which adds something to the output the customer eventually pays for. The rubber becomes a shoe component; the shoe component becomes an assembled shoe; the assembled shoe becomes a packaged product; the packaged product becomes an available item in a retail channel; the available item becomes a purchase decision informed by marketing.

At each stage, value is either added — through efficient production, smart logistics, effective marketing — or destroyed — through defects, waste, channel friction, poor customer experience. The total value the company captures is the cumulative result of how well it manages each stage and the connections between them.

This is not a complicated idea. What makes it powerful is its systematicity — the discipline it imposes of looking at every stage, not just the glamorous ones. Companies that have excellent products but terrible distribution fail. Companies that have great logistics but no marketing capability fail. Companies that have superb customer acquisition but catastrophic after-sales support fail more slowly, but they fail.

The value chain is a reminder that excellence in one stage cannot compensate for failure in another. In that sense it is one of the most honest frameworks in the entire toolkit — it does not let you look at the best parts of a business and avert your eyes from the rest.

## Exercises

**Warm-up**

1. A beverage company's gross margins have declined from 38% to 29% over three years. Using the seven-stage value chain, list the three stages you would investigate first and write one sentence for each explaining what cost behavior you'd expect to find there if that stage were the source of the problem. *(Tests: value chain as a search space — hypothesis formation before analysis)*

2. A factory is running at 55% capacity. Its fixed costs are $10 million per year and its variable costs are $8 per unit. If the factory runs at 80% capacity instead — assume maximum capacity is 200,000 units per year — calculate the change in fixed cost per unit. What does this tell you about the relationship between volume and manufacturing margins? *(Tests: fixed cost dynamics and capacity utilization)*

3. A defect in a shoe reaches the customer rather than being caught in manufacturing. The manufacturing cost of the unit is $18, the logistics cost is $4, and the after-sales service cost for handling a return and replacement is $22. What is the total cost of this defect compared to catching it in manufacturing? What additional cost category is not captured in these figures? *(Tests: defect multiplier effect across value chain stages)*

**Application**

4. A specialty food company sells through three channels: grocery retail (60% of volume), direct-to-consumer online (25%), and wholesale to restaurants (15%). Grocery retail requires a 35% margin share to the retailer. Online requires $18 per order in fulfillment and customer acquisition costs. Restaurant wholesale requires a dedicated sales team. Describe the margin profile of each channel and identify which channel you would examine first if the company's overall profitability was declining. Explain your reasoning. *(Tests: distribution channel analysis and margin trade-offs)*

5. A company is considering expanding its running shoe business from North America into Southeast Asia. Map the seven value chain stages and, for each stage, write one specific question you would ask to determine whether the company has the capability to operate in the new market or would need to build it. *(Tests: value chain applied to new market entry — capability audit)*

6. You are told that a consumer electronics company's direct costs are stable but its indirect costs have grown 40% over four years. Its revenue has grown only 12% over the same period. What type of structural problem does this pattern suggest? Which stages of the value chain are most likely to be sources of indirect cost accumulation, and what would you look for at each? *(Tests: direct vs. indirect cost distinction and structural diagnosis)*

**Synthesis**

7. A company has excellent product reviews, a strong brand, and high customer acquisition rates — but customer retention is poor and lifetime value is declining. Using the value chain, identify which stage or stages are most likely responsible and trace how a failure at those stages could produce this specific symptom pattern despite strong upstream performance. *(Tests: chain interdependence — how downstream failures manifest despite upstream excellence)*

8. A competitor has entered your client's market and is offering comparable products at 20% lower prices while maintaining profitability. The competitor manufactures in the same region. Using the value chain framework, generate three distinct hypotheses for where the competitor's cost advantage originates. For each hypothesis, identify the one piece of data that would confirm or rule it out. *(Tests: hypothesis-driven value chain analysis — weighting over auditing)*

**Challenge**

9. Porter's original value chain was designed for manufacturing firms. Choose a two-sided platform business (a ride-sharing company, a marketplace, a social network) and redraw the value chain to fit. Which of Porter's seven stages transfer directly, which require renaming and reinterpretation, and which have no analog? What new stages, if any, does a platform business require that Porter's model doesn't capture? *(Tests: framework adaptation — recognizing when a tool's assumptions don't fit and modifying it accordingly)*

## A Note About AI

Value chain analysis is a structured decomposition. The model decomposes fluently. The risk is producing a decomposition that looks correct and treats every link as equally weighted.

Where the model genuinely helps: producing the canonical value chain for an unfamiliar industry quickly, so you have a starting map before you begin to challenge it. The map is generic on purpose.

Where the model does damage: deciding which links in the value chain matter most for this specific case. The weighting is the analysis; the decomposition is just the setup.

The rule: structure from the model; weighting from you.
