# Chapter 15 — BCG Matrix, TAM/SAM/SOM, and Portfolio Strategy

In 1970, Boston Consulting Group published a two-by-two matrix on a single page. It had four cells, four animal names, and a deceptively simple premise: not all products deserve the same investment, and the mistake of treating them as if they do is one of the most reliable ways a company can destroy value.

The matrix was called the Growth-Share Matrix. You know it as the BCG Matrix, and the animals — Stars, Cash Cows, Question Marks, Dogs — have entered business vocabulary so completely that most people who use the terms have never thought carefully about the mechanics behind them. That is a problem, because the BCG Matrix is one of those frameworks that is easy to apply superficially and genuinely hard to apply well. The superficial application produces labels. The rigorous application produces resource allocation decisions that are either defensible or indefensible based on logic you can explain.

This chapter is about the logic.

---

## What the BCG Matrix Is Actually For

Before the mechanics, the purpose — because knowing what a tool is for is what separates using it from performing it.

The BCG Matrix is a portfolio management tool. That word "portfolio" is load-bearing. A portfolio is a collection of assets managed as a system, where the performance of each asset affects the resources available to invest in the others. A company with multiple products, business units, or strategic investments is managing a portfolio whether it thinks of itself that way or not. The BCG Matrix gives that portfolio management a structure.

The specific problem the matrix was designed to solve is the resource allocation problem under growth. Every company has finite capital. When some products are growing fast and some are growing slowly, and some have strong competitive positions and some have weak ones, how do you decide where to put the money? The intuitive answer — invest where you see the most opportunity — is incomplete, because opportunity is not the same as return. A high-growth market that requires massive investment to maintain competitive position may generate less return than a slow-growth market where the company has a commanding position and capital requirements are low.

The BCG Matrix makes this trade-off explicit by mapping each business unit or product along two dimensions that, together, determine the strategic logic of investment.

The first dimension is **market growth rate** — how fast is the market this product competes in growing? High growth means high opportunity, but also high investment requirements: to maintain share in a fast-growing market, a company must invest continuously just to keep pace with market expansion. Low growth means lower investment requirements but also limited revenue upside.

The second dimension is **relative market share** — how large is this product's market share compared to the largest competitor? Not absolute share, relative share. A product with 15% share in a market where the next competitor has 5% is in a fundamentally different competitive position than a product with 15% share where the leader has 40%. Relative market share is a proxy for competitive position and cost advantage: companies with higher relative share in a market typically have lower per-unit costs through learning curve effects and scale economies.

These two dimensions produce four quadrants. Each quadrant has a distinctive cash flow profile and a distinctive strategic logic.

---

## The Four Quadrants

### Stars: High Growth, High Share

Stars are products or business units that hold strong competitive positions in high-growth markets. They are the obvious strategic priority — the businesses you want to win.

The cash flow profile of a Star is more complicated than it first appears, and this is where many candidates go wrong. Stars are not necessarily cash generators. They are typically cash-neutral or cash-consuming, because the investment required to maintain competitive position in a high-growth market consumes the cash that the business's strong margins produce. You are running fast to stay in place.

Suppose a Star business generates $200 million in revenue with 25% operating margins — $50 million in operating profit. But to maintain its market share in a market growing at 30% annually, it needs to invest $60 million in capacity expansion, sales force growth, product development, and marketing. The business earns more than it consumes at maturity, but at this stage, it is a net consumer of cash at the portfolio level.

This is the first important insight the BCG Matrix produces: the businesses that look best on paper — high growth, strong position — may be exactly the businesses that drain the portfolio's cash reserves. The question is not whether to invest in Stars but whether the portfolio has a mechanism for generating the cash those investments require.

That mechanism, in the BCG framework, is the Cash Cow.

### Cash Cows: Low Growth, High Share

Cash Cows are products or business units that hold strong competitive positions in slow-growth markets. They are the portfolio's engine room.

The cash flow logic is the inverse of the Star. Because the market is growing slowly, the investment required to maintain market share is low — you don't need to invest massively just to keep pace with expansion. Because the competitive position is strong, margins are typically high. The result is a business that generates substantial cash with modest reinvestment requirements.

That cash is the portfolio's fuel. It funds the Stars that need investment to maintain their growth trajectory. It funds the Question Marks that might become the next generation of Stars. It returns capital to shareholders when the portfolio has more cash generation than investment opportunities.

The strategic mistake with Cash Cows is not underinvesting in them — it is over-investing in them. Because Cash Cows often represent a company's most established, most familiar products, the management team may feel attached to them and invest in revitalization initiatives that cannot generate attractive returns in a slow-growth market. Capital invested in a Cash Cow to boost market growth competes against capital invested in a Star to maintain market share in a fast-growing market. The returns almost never favor the Cash Cow.

The right posture for a Cash Cow is: harvest efficiently. Maintain the competitive position that generates the strong cash flows. Do not under-invest to the point of allowing the position to erode. But do not over-invest trying to grow a market that structural conditions don't support.

### Question Marks: High Growth, Low Share

Question Marks — sometimes called Problem Children — are products or business units that compete in high-growth markets but hold weak relative competitive positions. They are the portfolio's most difficult management challenge.

The cash flow logic is punishing. Because the market is growing fast, investment is required just to participate. Because the competitive position is weak, margins are typically lower than competitors', meaning the business generates less cash per unit than the leader does. The result is a business that consumes cash significantly and whose trajectory is genuinely uncertain.

The strategic decision with a Question Mark is binary, and this is exactly the decision the BCG Matrix forces into clarity: invest aggressively to build share and convert the Question Mark into a Star, or exit the market before the cash consumption exceeds any plausible return.

The "harvest" option that applies to Cash Cows and Dogs does not apply to Question Marks. In a high-growth market, standing still means falling behind. A competitor with strong market share and the learning curve advantages that come with it will outspend you, underprice you, and eventually push you to marginal relevance. Question Marks that are not converted into Stars become Dogs over time as the market matures — the growth rate falls, and the weak competitive position solidifies rather than improving.

What determines whether a Question Mark deserves the investment to become a Star? Two questions. First: does the company have a credible path to competitive parity or advantage in this market? Is there a strategy for building share — a product advantage, a distribution advantage, an acquisition, a partnership — that could plausibly close the gap with market leaders? Second: does the company have the capital and organizational capacity to make that investment without compromising the rest of the portfolio?

If the answer to either question is no, the rational decision is to exit. The emotional pull to stay in high-growth markets is powerful, but capital consumed by an unwinnable competitive battle in a Question Mark is capital not invested in Stars or not returned to shareholders.

### Dogs: Low Growth, Low Share

Dogs are products or business units with weak competitive positions in slow-growth markets. They are the portfolio's stranded assets.

The cash flow logic offers no refuge. The market is growing slowly, so there is no natural tailwind to improve the competitive position. The relative market share is low, so cost disadvantages relative to market leaders are structurally embedded. The business typically generates thin margins or, if the competitive dynamics are adverse, no margin at all.

The strategic options for Dogs are limited: harvest (extract whatever cash remains while allowing the position to erode further), divest (sell to a buyer who can create more value from the business than you can, perhaps a competitor for whom it would be a Cash Cow rather than a Dog), or liquidate.

The option that is almost never correct for a Dog is investment. Investing to build share in a slow-growth market requires taking share directly from competitors, which is expensive and provokes competitive response. Investing to accelerate market growth is typically beyond any individual company's ability. The capital almost always earns better returns elsewhere in the portfolio.

The emotional difficulty with Dogs is that they are often businesses with history — products that were once important to the company, markets where the company has long-standing relationships, activities where people have built careers. The BCG Matrix forces a clinical view: the question is not what this business has been, but what it will return on incremental capital invested in it from this point forward.

## The Cash Flow Logic as a System

The four quadrants work as a system, not as independent categories. Understanding the system is what makes the BCG Matrix genuinely analytical rather than just a labeling exercise.

The system operates through cash flow transfer. Cash Cows generate surplus cash. That surplus funds the investment in Stars required to maintain their competitive position through market growth. Some of that investment also flows to selected Question Marks that have credible paths to becoming Stars. The portfolio's overall cash position is the net of these flows.

A healthy portfolio has enough Cash Cow generation to fund the Star and selected Question Mark investments without requiring the company to raise external capital continuously. A portfolio that is out of balance — too many Stars and Question Marks relative to Cash Cows — will be continuously capital-hungry, requiring either equity dilution or debt financing to fund investments the portfolio can't self-fund.

The temporal dimension makes this more concrete. Stars don't stay Stars forever. As market growth rates slow — as the industry matures, as the underlying technology or behavior shift becomes mainstream — Stars migrate toward the Cash Cow quadrant. High relative market share persists; market growth rate falls. The Star becomes a Cash Cow, and the portfolio's cash generation capacity increases.

The portfolio manager's job is to maintain a pipeline: Question Marks that will become tomorrow's Stars, Stars that will become tomorrow's Cash Cows, and Cash Cows generating the capital that funds the whole progression. A portfolio with no Question Marks has no pipeline for future growth. A portfolio with no Cash Cows has no self-funding capacity for current investment. A portfolio weighted toward Dogs is liquidating its future.

## What the BCG Matrix Does Not Tell You

Honest treatment of any framework requires acknowledging what it doesn't capture, not just what it does.

The BCG Matrix makes two assumptions that are strong enough to be worth naming explicitly. The first is that market growth rate is the right proxy for investment attractiveness. In most industries over most time periods, this assumption holds — fast-growing markets do generally offer more attractive investment opportunities than slow-growing ones. But there are exceptions. A slow-growing market with enormous barriers to entry, high switching costs, and pricing power may offer better return on investment than a fast-growing market that is structurally competitive and likely to commoditize. Market growth rate is a useful heuristic, not an infallible rule.

The second assumption is that relative market share is the right proxy for competitive position. This assumption rests on the learning curve effect — the empirical observation that costs per unit fall predictably as cumulative production volume increases. In manufacturing-intensive industries, this relationship is strong and well-documented. In industries where competitive advantage comes primarily from intellectual property, brand, network effects, or relationships rather than production scale, the connection between market share and cost advantage is weaker. A professional services firm with 5% market share might be more profitably positioned than one with 25% share, if the 5% firm focuses on the highest-value clients and the 25% firm pursues volume at the expense of margin.

Neither of these limitations invalidates the framework. They specify the conditions under which its assumptions are most reliable and flag the adaptations required when those conditions don't fully hold.

The BCG Matrix also says nothing about the quality of the competitive position — only its magnitude. Two businesses with the same relative market share might have radically different competitive durability, depending on whether that share is protected by moats or is structurally fragile. Chapter 11's due diligence framework addresses that question; the BCG Matrix does not. In practice, strong portfolio analysis uses both: the BCG Matrix for resource allocation logic, and a competitive moat assessment for evaluating the durability of each position.

## Market Sizing: Why TAM, SAM, and SOM Belong Here

Portfolio strategy is impossible without market sizing. The BCG Matrix asks about market growth rate and relative market share — both of which require a clear definition of the market being measured. Without that definition, every number is contestable and every strategic conclusion is unstable.

Market sizing also enters portfolio analysis through a different door: the assessment of Question Marks. The decision to invest in converting a Question Mark into a Star requires a view on the total value of the prize — the total market the company is competing for. Investing heavily to build share in a large, rapidly growing market is a different decision than investing heavily in a small, rapidly growing market. TAM, SAM, and SOM provide the vocabulary for making that distinction precise.

The three market measures form a hierarchy, and understanding the hierarchy is as important as understanding the definitions.

### TAM: Total Addressable Market

TAM is the total annual revenue opportunity available for a product or service if the company captured 100% of the market — every potential customer, every geography, every use case.

TAM is not a realistic target. It is a theoretical ceiling. No company captures 100% of its TAM, ever. The value of TAM is not as a forecast but as a boundary condition: it tells you the maximum size of the opportunity and therefore the maximum scale of the prize worth competing for.

The calculation of TAM requires a clear definition of the market and a choice of methodology. Two approaches are standard.

**Top-down methodology** starts from macro-level data — population statistics, industry reports, macroeconomic measures — and applies penetration and spend assumptions to arrive at a market size. For a consumer product, the logic might proceed: there are 250 million adults in the United States. Roughly 40% have characteristics that make them potential users of this product. Average annual spend in similar categories is $200 per user. TAM = 250M × 0.40 × $200 = $20 billion.

Each step requires a judgment about how to define the relevant population and what spending rate to assume. These judgments are the model's key assumptions, and they should be made explicit and tested. What if the penetrable population is 30% rather than 40%? What if average spend is $150 rather than $200? The sensitivity analysis around these assumptions tells you how confident the TAM estimate actually is.

**Bottom-up methodology** starts from the customer unit and builds up. It begins by identifying the specific customer segments that would use the product, estimating the number of customers in each segment, and estimating revenue per customer. For a B2B software product, the logic might proceed: there are 50,000 companies in the target segment. Average contract value is $30,000 annually. TAM = 50,000 × $30,000 = $1.5 billion.

Bottom-up methodology is often more rigorous for B2B markets where customer counts can be estimated from industry databases or surveys. It is harder to execute for consumer markets where the relevant population is less easily enumerated.

In case analysis, doing both and checking for convergence is the strongest approach. When top-down and bottom-up estimates converge within a reasonable range, you have a credible TAM estimate. When they diverge significantly, the divergence is telling you something about which assumptions are most consequential and where additional research would most improve the estimate's accuracy.

### SAM: Serviceable Addressable Market

SAM is the portion of TAM that the company can realistically serve given its current business model, go-to-market strategy, and geographic footprint.

The move from TAM to SAM is the move from theoretical to realistic opportunity. It applies the constraints of current capability and reach. A company that sells through direct enterprise sales has a SAM that is bounded by the size and productivity of its sales force. A company that operates only in the United States has a SAM that is bounded by U.S. market dynamics, regardless of the global TAM. A product that requires high-speed broadband internet has a SAM that excludes the portion of the TAM in geographies without broadband access.

SAM is the market the company's current strategy is actually competing for. It is the relevant denominator for current market share calculations. When the BCG Matrix asks about relative market share, the relevant comparison is share of SAM, not share of TAM — because the parts of TAM the company cannot currently serve don't affect the competitive dynamics of the segments where it actually competes.

The gap between TAM and SAM is strategically significant because it represents the additional market that could become accessible if the company extended its capabilities, geography, or business model. A company that has captured 25% of a $500 million SAM might see a $2 billion TAM and recognize that 75% of the total opportunity is outside its current reach. That gap is not a problem to ignore — it is the pipeline of potential market development moves, each requiring specific capability investments to unlock.

### SOM: Serviceable Obtainable Market

SOM is the portion of SAM that the company can realistically capture within a defined time horizon, given its current competitive position, resources, and execution capacity.

The move from SAM to SOM is the move from realistic to achievable opportunity. It applies the further constraint of competitive dynamics and execution capacity. Even within the SAM the company can serve, it doesn't capture 100% — competitors exist, customer acquisition takes time and investment, and sales cycles constrain how quickly new customers can be added.

SOM is the market the current strategic plan is actually targeting. It is the basis for revenue forecasts, sales force sizing, and investment planning. When a company sets a growth target of 15% annual revenue growth, that target should be grounded in a SOM analysis that shows 15% growth is achievable given the competitive dynamics of the SAM.

The calculation of SOM is essentially a market share projection within the SAM. It requires assumptions about the company's share of the SAM today, the rate at which that share will change, and the underlying growth of the SAM itself. These assumptions should be benchmarked against historical share change rates in this and comparable markets, not generated optimistically from strategic intent.

## Using TAM, SAM, SOM in Case Analysis

The three-level market sizing framework is most valuable when it is used dynamically rather than as a static snapshot. The interesting strategic questions are in the transitions between levels.

**The TAM-to-SAM gap** reveals the constraints on the current business model. If a company's SAM is only 20% of its TAM, the strategic question is: what would it cost to expand the SAM, and what would the return on that expansion be? The answer involves identifying the specific constraints — geographic reach, product capability, channel access, customer segment focus — that define the current SAM and asking whether each constraint is surmountable and at what investment.

This analysis often surfaces the most valuable strategic options for a Question Mark. A Question Mark in a large TAM with a small SAM may have a credible path to becoming a Star not by competing harder in its current SAM but by expanding the SAM to include market segments where its competitive position would be stronger or where the competition is less entrenched.

**The SAM-to-SOM gap** reveals the competitive efficiency of the current go-to-market approach. If a company's SOM is only 5% of its SAM, either the competitive position is weak (competitors are winning most of the available opportunity) or the go-to-market reach is insufficient (the company isn't reaching all the customers it could serve). Distinguishing between these two explanations requires different remedies: competitive position problems require product and pricing work, go-to-market reach problems require distribution and sales capacity investment.

**The SOM growth rate** relative to SAM growth rate tells you whether the company is gaining or losing share within its addressable market. A company growing SOM at 25% in a SAM growing at 20% is gaining share — a positive signal. A company growing SOM at 10% in a SAM growing at 20% is losing share — a negative signal regardless of how the absolute revenue growth looks in isolation.

## The Integration: BCG Matrix Informed by TAM/SAM/SOM

The BCG Matrix and the TAM/SAM/SOM framework are most powerful when used together, because they address complementary questions that neither answers alone.

The BCG Matrix asks: given current competitive position and market growth, where should investment go? TAM/SAM/SOM asks: how big is the prize worth competing for, and how realistic is the current share trajectory?

The integration produces a more complete portfolio analysis. Consider a company evaluating a Question Mark:

The BCG Matrix says: this business is consuming cash and has weak relative market share in a fast-growing market. The binary decision is invest-to-win or exit.

TAM/SAM/SOM adds: the TAM for this market is $15 billion. The company's current SAM is $800 million — it only addresses about 5% of the TAM. Within that SAM, the company has 12% share (SOM of roughly $100 million). The market leader has 35% share in the same SAM.

Now the investment decision has content. Closing the gap from 12% to 25% share in a $800 million SAM requires gaining roughly $100 million in additional revenue — achievable in principle, but demanding in competitive terms. However, the TAM-to-SAM gap reveals that $14.2 billion of the total market is currently unaddressed by the company's business model. If the business model could be extended to serve adjacent segments, the SAM could triple, and the company's existing 12% share within its current SAM would represent a much stronger foundation for growth.

The invest-to-win decision looks different when the opportunity is framed this way. The question is not just "can we close the share gap in the current SAM?" but "is the right strategic move to expand the SAM where our competitive position would be stronger, before trying to close the gap against a dominant leader in the narrow market we currently compete in?"

That is the kind of strategic insight that emerges when the BCG Matrix and TAM/SAM/SOM are used as a system.

## Portfolio Strategy: What the Frameworks Are Building Toward

Individual product assessments are the inputs. Portfolio strategy is the output.

Portfolio strategy is the answer to a single question: given what we know about each element of our portfolio, how do we allocate capital and management attention across the portfolio to maximize total return over the relevant time horizon?

The BCG Matrix and TAM/SAM/SOM give you the analytical foundation for answering that question. But the answer requires one more layer of judgment that no framework can fully supply: the sequencing of investment and the management of interdependencies between portfolio elements.

**Sequencing** matters because the order in which investments are made affects the resources available for subsequent investments. A company that over-invests in Stars too early — before its Cash Cows are generating adequate surplus — may find itself capital-constrained precisely when Question Marks need investment to become Stars. The right sequencing ensures that the portfolio's cash generation capacity is always at least one step ahead of its investment demands.

**Interdependencies** matter because portfolio elements often affect each other in ways that individual-product analysis misses. A Star in one product category may create distribution relationships that make a Question Mark in an adjacent category easier to convert. A Cash Cow's customer base may be the most natural early-adopter market for a new Star. A Dog's technology assets, divested intelligently, might fund a Question Mark investment.

These interdependencies are why portfolio strategy is genuinely strategic rather than merely financial. The BCG Matrix produces labels. Portfolio strategy produces a plan for how the portfolio evolves over time — which elements grow, which are harvested, which are exited, and in what sequence — in a way that is internally consistent and financially self-sustaining.

## How to Use These Frameworks in a Case

Portfolio analysis cases appear in several forms, and recognizing the form matters for knowing which tool to apply first.

When the case presents a company with multiple business units and asks about strategic priorities or resource allocation, reach for the BCG Matrix first. Map each unit to a quadrant based on relative market share and market growth rate. Identify the cash flow implications of each quadrant. Then ask whether the portfolio is balanced: does it have enough Cash Cow generation to fund the Stars and selected Question Marks? Is the portfolio building a pipeline from Question Marks toward future Stars?

When the case involves a specific investment decision — should we enter this market, should we invest to build share in this product category, how large is the opportunity in this segment — reach for TAM/SAM/SOM. Build the market sizing from both top-down and bottom-up. Identify the constraints that define the SAM. Estimate the SOM based on realistic share trajectory. Check whether the prize is large enough to justify the investment.

When the case involves a portfolio and a specific investment decision simultaneously, the integration becomes the analysis. The BCG Matrix tells you the strategic posture of the specific product or business unit under consideration. TAM/SAM/SOM tells you the size and realism of the opportunity. Together, they produce a resource allocation recommendation that is grounded in both competitive logic and market reality.

The common failure mode in portfolio cases is performing the BCG Matrix as a labeling exercise — categorizing each unit and describing its strategic implications — without connecting the analysis to a concrete resource allocation recommendation. The labels are inputs. The recommendation is the output. A Star needs continued investment to maintain position. How much investment, and funded by what? A Question Mark needs a binary decision. Based on the TAM/SAM/SOM analysis, what does the SOM analysis imply about the feasibility of the win-or-exit decision?

Every portfolio analysis should end with a clear view on: where investment increases, where it holds, where it decreases, and how the portfolio's overall cash dynamics support the allocation. That is the recommendation the client actually needs.

## The Logic Underneath

The BCG Matrix was developed at a moment when capital allocation was genuinely poorly understood in most large corporations. The dominant practice was to allocate capital to business units based on historical spending patterns, internal political dynamics, and the relative persuasiveness of business unit managers — not based on the cash flow logic of each unit's competitive and market position.

What BCG introduced was a discipline: the recognition that different businesses within the same portfolio have fundamentally different relationships to cash, and that treating them identically produces systematic misallocation. Stars consume cash despite their strong positions. Cash Cows generate cash despite their unexciting growth rates. Question Marks consume cash with uncertain futures. Dogs consume or at best conserve cash with poor return prospects.

The discipline of the matrix is forcing that recognition into explicit resource allocation decisions. Once you have mapped a business unit to a quadrant, you cannot avoid the question of whether your current investment level is appropriate for that quadrant's cash flow logic. You might decide the framework's prescription is wrong for your specific situation — and sometimes it is, for the reasons discussed in the limitations section. But at least you are making that decision consciously, with the framework's logic in front of you, rather than by default.

That is what good analytical frameworks do. They don't replace judgment. They make judgment explicit, force it to engage with specific evidence, and create accountability for the reasoning behind decisions.

The BCG Matrix has survived fifty years not because it is perfect but because it captures something true about how portfolios work — and because the discipline of facing the cash flow logic of each business unit is genuinely valuable, regardless of how many times people have abused the framework by applying it mechanically to situations its assumptions don't fit.

Use it as a system. Pair it with TAM/SAM/SOM when the size of the opportunity matters to the investment decision. And always remember that the goal is not to correctly classify your portfolio. The goal is to allocate capital in the way that best serves the company's long-term competitive position.

The matrix is the means. The allocation is the end.

## Chapter Summary

The BCG Matrix is a portfolio management tool, not a product analysis tool. Its power is in forcing explicit recognition of the cash flow logic that differs across competitive positions and market growth rates — and therefore in making resource allocation decisions based on that logic rather than on political dynamics or historical spending patterns.

Stars consume cash to maintain their position. Cash Cows generate cash surplus. Question Marks are binary bets: invest to win or exit before the losses exceed the option value. Dogs are stranded assets to be harvested, divested, or liquidated.

TAM, SAM, and SOM provide the market sizing foundation that gives BCG Matrix decisions their quantitative grounding. TAM defines the theoretical ceiling. SAM defines the realistic opportunity given current capabilities. SOM defines the achievable target given competitive dynamics. The gaps between each level reveal strategic options that portfolio analysis alone doesn't surface.

The Feynman test for this chapter: can you explain to someone why a company with excellent, profitable products might still be making a capital allocation mistake? If the answer involves Stars, Cash Cows, and the cash flow logic of the portfolio — not just the attractiveness of individual products — you have the idea.

The most common mistake in portfolio analysis is performing the classification without performing the allocation. Labels without implications are decoration. The BCG Matrix earns its analytical weight when it produces specific investment decisions: this business gets more capital, this one holds steady, this one gets harvested, this one gets exited — and here is the cash flow logic that makes each decision defensible.

That discipline is the point. Everything else is vocabulary.

## A note about AI

Portfolio strategy is the level where the BCG matrix meets the real resource-allocation question. The model can write portfolio analyses fluently and finish without a recommendation.

Where the model genuinely helps: producing the canonical trade-offs in a multi-business-unit portfolio — investment, harvest, divestiture — as a vocabulary you can deploy.

Where the model does damage: prescribing what to do across the portfolio. The prescription is the test of whether you can commit under uncertainty, which is what the case is measuring.

The rule: vocabulary from the model; prescription from you.
