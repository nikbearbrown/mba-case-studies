# Chapter 5 — Market Entry Framework

*Why the bet has two parts, and why collapsing them is the most expensive mistake in strategy.*

---

Every market entry decision is, at its core, a bet.

The company is wagering capital, management attention, organizational energy, and often a piece of its existing business against the proposition that a new market will reward the investment. Sometimes the bet is modest — a small product line extension into an adjacent geography, low stakes, reversible if it doesn't work. Sometimes it is enormous — a full greenfield build in a foreign country, years of losses before profitability, a commitment so large that failure would damage the parent company seriously.

What makes market entry analytically interesting is that the bet has two completely separate components that candidates routinely collapse into one. The first is whether the market is worth entering at all — whether the economics and competitive dynamics make it attractive enough to justify the investment. The second is how to enter — which specific mode gives the company the best chance of success given its capabilities and the market's characteristics.

Collapsing these two questions produces bad analysis. A company can correctly identify that a market is attractive and then destroy value by entering it the wrong way. Conversely, a company can develop a brilliant entry strategy for a market that isn't actually worth entering. The two questions require different analytical tools and different kinds of evidence. Treating them as one means you will answer neither well.

This chapter is about how to answer both.

---

## What You Need to Know Before You Touch a Framework

There is a discipline in market entry analysis that separates strong candidates from weak ones, and it happens before any framework gets applied. It is the discipline of understanding what the company actually wants and why.

"Enter a new market" is not a strategy. It is an intention. Behind that intention is a motivation, and the motivation matters enormously because it determines what success looks like and therefore what analysis is required.

A company might be considering market entry because its domestic market has saturated and growth requires new geography. That is a growth imperative. Or because a competitor has moved into the target market and the company needs a defensive presence. That is a competitive response. Or because the company has a product uniquely suited to conditions in the target market — regulatory environment, demographic profile, unmet need. That is opportunity exploitation. Or because investors are demanding a geographic diversification story. That is a financial narrative requirement that may or may not correspond to genuine strategic logic.

Each motivation implies a different threshold for what counts as a sufficient return. A defensive market entry might be worth executing even at breakeven, if the cost of ceding the market to a competitor is high enough. An opportunistic entry into an attractive market might require a higher return threshold precisely because the company has no urgency. A growth-imperative entry needs to actually deliver growth, which means market size and growth rate matter more than margins, at least in the short term.

So before you touch a framework, ask: what is driving the decision to consider this? And what does success look like from the client's perspective? These two questions will orient every subsequent piece of analysis.

The second piece of setup is understanding the company itself — its product portfolio, its operational model, its experience with previous market entries, its capabilities along the value chain. A company that has successfully entered ten new markets has organizational muscle memory that a company making its first international move does not have. A company that makes premium products faces a different challenge entering a price-sensitive emerging market than a company that has always competed on cost. A company that manages its own distribution at home may find that capability transfers — or may find that local distribution networks are entirely relationship-dependent in ways that make organic entry nearly impossible.

These are not details to be addressed after the strategic decision. They are inputs to the strategic decision.

## Is the Market Worth Entering?

With the setup done, the first major analytical question is attractiveness: should the company enter this market at all?

The analysis has four dimensions, and each asks a distinct question.

The first is the customer question: who is actually in this market, and what do they want? This means segmenting the potential customer base — not just by demographics, but by what customers are trying to accomplish and what they currently use to accomplish it. It means estimating market size and growth rate. It means identifying the target segment most likely to adopt the company's offering and being honest about what share of that segment the company could realistically capture, and over what timeframe.

Market sizing deserves care because the method matters. A top-down approach starts from macro data — total population, income distribution, category spend as a percentage of income — and works down to an addressable segment. A bottom-up approach starts from the customer unit and builds up: if we can reach X distribution points and each point generates Y customers at Z revenue each, we arrive at a total. Neither method is inherently superior; the right choice depends on what data is available and which direction of estimation carries fewer uncertain assumptions. In case interviews, doing both and triangulating between them is a powerful demonstration of analytical rigor.

The second dimension is the company question: is this company specifically equipped to win in this market? This runs through the value chain. Can the company procure inputs cost-effectively for this market? Can it manufacture products that meet this market's requirements? Can it build or access distribution that reaches target customers? Does it have the brand presence or marketing capability to acquire customers in an unfamiliar environment? Each stage of the value chain may need to be built, acquired, or partnered into existence, and the cost and feasibility of doing so is a major input to the entry decision.

The financial analysis sits here too. The core calculation is straightforward: projected revenue minus projected costs equals projected profit, and projected profit minus investment required to enter equals projected return. The question is whether the return exceeds the company's cost of capital by enough to justify the risk. In practice, this calculation requires assumptions about market share capture rate, pricing levels, and cost structure — assumptions that need to be stress-tested. What do the economics look like if market share capture takes twice as long as projected? What if local cost inputs are 20% higher than estimated? The range of outcomes, not just the base case, is what tells you whether the market is really worth entering.

The third dimension is the competition question: who is already in the market, and what would it cost to displace or coexist with them? The relevant analysis covers number of competitors and market share concentration, the competitive advantages of existing players, barriers to entry and exit, and the regulatory environment. A market with three entrenched competitors who have deep local relationships and regulatory protections is categorically different from a fragmented market where no player has dominant share. The first is difficult to enter and incumbent response to new entry will be aggressive. The second may be genuinely open.

Barriers to entry deserve particular attention because they are asymmetric. Some barriers make entry hard for everyone. Others specifically disadvantage companies without local presence, local relationships, or local regulatory approval. A foreign company entering a market where government contracts dominate faces barriers that a local company does not. A company entering a market where the dominant competitor owns exclusive distribution relationships faces barriers that product quality or price advantage alone cannot overcome.

The fourth dimension is the product question: is there actually a gap between what customers want and what they currently have? This question is deceptively important and frequently skipped.

A market can look attractive on every other dimension — large, growing, underpenetrated, lightly defended — and still be a poor entry target if customers are broadly satisfied with existing offerings. Switching costs are real. The inertia of established habits is real. A company entering with a product that is incrementally better than the incumbent offering faces a harder adoption challenge than one entering with a product that addresses a genuine unmet need — something customers want and cannot currently get. The gap analysis requires understanding not just what customers currently use, but what they complain about, what workarounds they have developed, what they would use if it existed.

## How to Enter: The Mode Decision

If the attractiveness analysis concludes that the market is worth entering, the second question is how. The four main entry modes — acquisition, greenfield build, joint venture, and licensing — have fundamentally different risk profiles, cost structures, and capability requirements.

Acquisition means buying an existing player in the target market. The advantage is speed: you acquire market presence, customer relationships, local brand equity, distribution networks, and operational capability in a single transaction. The disadvantages are cost — acquisitions require paying a premium over book value — integration risk, and the risk that what you are acquiring is not actually what it appeared to be during due diligence.

Acquisition makes most sense when time is a critical constraint, when competitive dynamics are moving fast enough that organic entry would be too slow, or when the capabilities required to compete would take too long to build. It also makes sense when an attractive target exists — a specific company whose assets, relationships, or capabilities are worth more to the entering company than their standalone value.

The key question about any acquisition is whether the value being acquired is truly transferable. Brand equity in a local market often depends on the founding team or existing management — if they leave post-acquisition, the brand equity may leave with them. Customer relationships may be personal rather than institutional. Regulatory licenses may not transfer. What you see in the target may not be what you get after the deal closes.

Greenfield means building from scratch — establishing new operations, hiring local talent, building distribution relationships, investing in brand awareness, developing local supply chains. The advantage is control: you build exactly what you want, with the culture and processes and capabilities you intend. The disadvantages are time and cost. A greenfield build typically takes years to reach operating efficiency and longer still to reach profitability. During that time, the company is absorbing losses and management attention, and the market may move in ways that erode the original investment thesis.

Greenfield makes sense when the company has sufficient financial staying power to absorb the investment period, when there is no suitable acquisition target, when control over the operation is strategically important, and when the company's competitive advantage is deeply embedded in its processes and culture — things that would be diluted or corrupted by acquiring a local incumbent with different ways of operating.

Joint venture means partnering with a local entity to share the investment, risk, and eventually the returns from market entry. The local partner contributes market knowledge, relationships, regulatory standing, and distribution access. The entering company contributes capital, product, technology, or brand. The advantage over greenfield is that the local partner's knowledge dramatically reduces the learning curve. The advantage over acquisition is that you are not paying full price for capabilities you are only partially leveraging.

The disadvantages of joint ventures are well-documented and serious. Governance is inherently complex when two organizations with different cultures, different incentive structures, and potentially different long-term objectives share control. What happens when the joint venture needs more capital and the partners disagree about how much to invest? What happens when one partner wants to expand into adjacent markets and the other does not? Joint ventures have a high failure rate relative to their strategic promise, and the most common reason is not commercial failure but governance failure. They require significant investment in governance design upfront — clear decision rights, explicit provisions for capital calls, agreed exit mechanisms — to have a reasonable chance of working.

Licensing represents the lightest-touch entry option. The company licenses its technology, brand, or product design to a local operator, who manufactures and distributes under a contractual arrangement. The entering company receives royalties without bearing the capital cost of building local operations.

The appeal is obvious: low investment, low risk, capital-efficient exposure to the market. The problem is equally obvious: low control. The licensee is running the business, and their incentives may not align with the licensor's. If the licensee cuts costs in ways that damage product quality, the entering company's brand suffers. If the licensee builds distribution relationships that give them bargaining leverage in renegotiating terms, the entering company's economics deteriorate. Licensing is often a market test — a low-commitment way of establishing presence before deciding whether to invest more substantially — rather than a permanent strategic position.

## The Entry Decision as a System

The entry mode decision cannot be made independently of the attractiveness analysis, and neither can be made independently of the company's strategic intent and capability profile. These three components — intent, attractiveness, and entry mode — form a system, and the analysis has to hold together across all three.

Consider a company with a highly differentiated product and strong manufacturing capabilities but no experience with international logistics or local market knowledge. The attractiveness analysis might show that a particular market is genuinely attractive. But greenfield would require building distribution and local knowledge from scratch, which takes too long. Acquisition requires finding a target whose distribution assets are strong enough to justify the premium. Joint venture might be best — it provides the local knowledge the company lacks — but only if the company can find a partner whose capabilities genuinely complement its own rather than overlap with them.

The analysis is not complete until these pieces connect. The conclusion is not "this market is attractive" or "use a joint venture." It is: this market is attractive because of X and Y, the company can compete because of Z, the preferred entry mode is joint venture because the company lacks local distribution and the regulatory environment requires local partnership, and the financial return under that mode at realistic market share assumptions exceeds the cost of capital by a sufficient margin to justify the risk.

That is a recommendation. Everything before it is analysis.

## What Market Entry Analysis Is Actually Testing

In a case interview, market entry cases are testing something specific: whether you can think simultaneously about external attractiveness and internal capability, and whether you understand that the right answer is not always yes.

Many candidates, especially those trained on growth strategy, approach market entry cases with an implicit bias toward recommending entry. The market looks attractive, the company has some relevant capabilities, there is a reasonable entry mode — let's go. This is almost always wrong, or at least incomplete. The interesting analytical work is in the cases where the market looks attractive but the company's capability gaps make entry inadvisable, or where the market is only moderately attractive but the company has such a strong position that even modest returns would be excellent.

The other thing being tested is quantitative judgment. Market entry cases almost always have a financial component — you need to estimate market size, project market share, model the economics of a specific entry mode, calculate a breakeven point. These calculations require assumptions, and the assumptions need to be internally consistent and defensible. A market share projection of 25% in year one is almost never defensible for a new entrant in a competitive market, regardless of how differentiated the product is. A positive return calculated using optimistic assumptions on every variable is not evidence that the market is worth entering — it is evidence that optimistic assumptions produce optimistic results.

The market entry framework is ultimately a structure for disciplined optimism: being enthusiastic about opportunities while being honest about what they require and what might go wrong. Companies that enter markets for clear reasons, with honest eyes about the challenges, executing the right entry mode for their specific capabilities, tend to succeed. Companies that enter because the market looks big and the framework said yes tend to fail in predictable ways.

The analysis should prepare you to tell the difference.

### Application

**4.** The US apparel company has strong manufacturing capabilities and an established premium brand at home, but no international distribution experience and no local relationships in Brazil. Using the four dimensions from this chapter, identify which dimension represents the company's biggest analytical uncertainty and explain why. *(Tests: applying the attractiveness framework to a specific capability profile rather than generically.)*

**5.** Construct a top-down market sizing estimate for the addressable premium segment of the Brazilian athletic apparel market. State your starting assumptions explicitly, identify the two assumptions with the most uncertainty, and explain how you would stress-test them. *(Tests: market sizing method — top-down construction with honest uncertainty mapping.)*

**6.** The attractiveness analysis comes back positive: the market is large, growing, underpenetrated in the premium segment, and the company's product addresses a genuine gap. Now evaluate which entry mode — acquisition, greenfield, joint venture, or licensing — best fits this company's capability profile. State which mode you recommend and the specific reason its risk profile fits better than the alternatives. *(Tests: entry mode selection grounded in capability analysis, not generic comparison.)*

**7.** A joint venture partner has been identified: a large Brazilian sportswear retailer with strong national distribution and government relationships, but whose house brand competes directly in the mid-market segment. Identify the governance risks this partnership creates and explain what provisions you would want in the joint venture agreement to address them. *(Tests: joint venture governance failure modes applied to a specific scenario.)*

### Synthesis

**8.** The company's CFO says: "Our base case shows a 14% IRR on the joint venture, which clears our 12% hurdle rate — so we're in." ## A Note About AI

Market entry questions are where the model is most fluently misleading. The framework is well-known, so the model produces confident output. The output rarely engages with the entry-specific friction that makes real cases hard.

Where the model genuinely helps: producing the standard market-entry checklist — size, growth, competition, channels, regulation, capabilities — faster than you can recall it. Useful as a memory aid.

Where the model does damage: writing the answer to a specific market entry case. The specifics — your client's actual capabilities, the local regulatory environment, the channel partners that actually exist — are not in the model's training in any actionable way.

The rule: framework from the model, judgment from you.
