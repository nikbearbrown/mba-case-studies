# Chapter 8 — Pricing Framework

Here is a question that sounds simple and isn't: what is something worth?

Not what does it cost to make. Not what does the seller want for it. What is it actually worth — to the specific person buying it, in the specific moment they're deciding whether to buy?

A bottle of water at a grocery store is worth about a dollar. The same bottle of water at the end of a long hike, when you're dehydrated and the nearest store is six miles back, is worth considerably more. The water is identical. The molecules haven't changed. What changed is the buyer's situation — the alternatives available, the urgency of the need, the cost of going without. The value of the thing is not in the thing. It is in the relationship between the thing and the person who needs it.

This is the foundational insight of pricing, and almost everything that follows in this chapter is a consequence of it. Price is not a number attached to a product. Price is the mechanism by which a seller captures a fraction of the value a buyer receives. Set it too low and the seller leaves value on the table — they've transferred wealth to the buyer unnecessarily. Set it too high and the buyer doesn't purchase — the deal that would have benefited both parties doesn't happen. Pricing is the art of finding where in that range the transaction should occur.

The reason pricing cases appear in consulting interviews is that this art is genuinely difficult, genuinely consequential, and genuinely analytical. A one-percentage-point improvement in price, for a company with thin margins, can increase profits by 10 or 15 or 20 percent. No operational efficiency, no cost reduction program, no volume initiative comes close to the leverage that pricing has on the bottom line. Companies routinely leave enormous amounts of money on the table by pricing too low, and occasionally destroy themselves by pricing too high. Getting it right requires exactly the kind of structured analysis that consulting develops.

## Before the Methodology: What Are You Actually Pricing?

The single most important question in a pricing case is not "what methodology should we use?" It is "what kind of product are we pricing, and what does that tell us about the relevant approach?"

This matters because pricing methodology is not universal. The approach appropriate for a genuinely novel product — something that has never existed before and that customers have no reference point for — is completely different from the approach appropriate for a product that enters an established competitive market where customers have multiple alternatives they're already familiar with. Applying the wrong methodology doesn't just produce an imprecise answer; it produces an answer oriented toward the wrong considerations.

Four product categories determine the analytical emphasis, and identifying which category you're in is the first decision in any pricing case.

**A new invention** is a product that creates a category. Before the iPhone, there was no market for smartphones as we now understand them. Before Airbnb, there was no market for peer-to-peer short-term home rental at scale. When a product creates a category, customers have no existing reference price. They don't know what something like this "should" cost because nothing like this has existed. The pricing methodology that applies here is almost entirely value-based: you have to understand what the product is worth to the customer — what problem it solves, what alternatives it replaces, how much those alternatives cost — because cost and competition provide almost no guidance.

**A modification to an existing product** is something that improves on what already exists — faster, more durable, more accurate, more convenient — but doesn't create a new category. The existing product sets the customer's reference price, and the question becomes: how much is the improvement worth above that reference? A drug that reduces hospital stays by two days is worth something calculable if you know what hospital stays cost. A manufacturing machine that increases throughput by 20% is worth something calculable if you know the value of that incremental output. The value-based approach still applies, but now there's an anchor — the cost of the alternative the product replaces or improves on.

**A product similar to existing offerings** is the most competitive pricing situation. The customer has several alternatives, roughly comparable in function, and price becomes a primary differentiator. Here, competitor-based pricing is the dominant methodology: you must understand what comparable products charge and what your product offers differently, and price to reflect that differential accurately. Pricing too far above comparable products requires a value justification; pricing too far below signals either inferior quality or an unsustainable attempt to buy market share.

**Pricing relative to another product in your own portfolio** is a different problem altogether. Here the question is not how to maximize value capture on a single product but how to price the portfolio to maximize overall profitability without cannibalizing your own higher-margin products. This requires understanding the cross-price elasticities between your products — how much a price change on one affects demand for another — and designing a portfolio architecture where each tier serves a distinct customer segment without undermining the others.

These four categories are not perfectly discrete in practice. A product might be novel enough to require value-based thinking while also having competitive alternatives that set a rough ceiling on price. But the taxonomy is useful because it identifies which type of evidence is most important: value-based evidence (what is the product worth to the buyer?), cost-based evidence (can we price to cover costs and generate return?), or competitor-based evidence (what does the market currently accept?). Knowing which evidence type is most important tells you where to focus your analysis.

## The Three Methodologies

With the product category established, the three pricing methodologies can be applied with appropriate emphasis.

### Value-Based Pricing

Value-based pricing starts from the customer's perspective and works backwards to a price. The core question is: what is this product worth to the buyer? And that question breaks into two subsidiary questions that must be answered separately before they can be combined.

The first is **willingness to pay**: the maximum price at which the customer would purchase rather than go without or seek an alternative. This is not what the customer thinks is "fair." It is not what the customer would prefer to pay. It is the threshold above which the customer says no. Willingness to pay varies across customer segments — the same product may be worth much more to a large enterprise than to a small one, much more to a customer who faces an urgent need than to one who has flexibility. Understanding the distribution of willingness to pay across the customer population is essential for deciding both what price to charge and how to segment pricing across customer types.

The second is **opportunity cost**, or more precisely, the value of the customer's best alternative. What does the customer use now to accomplish the same goal? What does that alternative cost, in money and in other ways — time, effort, quality shortfalls? The difference between what the customer's current alternative costs and what your product costs is the value surplus you're delivering. Willingness to pay is ultimately bounded by this value surplus: a rational customer won't pay more for your product than it costs them to use the alternative, unless your product delivers something the alternative genuinely cannot.

In practice, willingness-to-pay and opportunity cost analysis often require estimation rather than direct observation. Customers don't always tell you their true willingness to pay — they have strategic reasons to understate it. And opportunity costs are sometimes hard to quantify because they include non-monetary components like time, inconvenience, or quality risk. The analytical work here is in constructing defensible proxies: comparable transactions, economic modeling of customer value, analysis of switching costs.

Supply and demand dynamics constrain value-based pricing from the market side. Even if the product delivers enormous value to customers, a competitive market where multiple suppliers can provide it will drive price toward cost as competition intensifies. Value-based pricing works best — and is most defensible as a pricing strategy — when the product has some form of protection against competitive imitation: patent protection, trade secrets, proprietary data, network effects, or switching costs that make competition slow to erode the pricing power.

### Cost-Plus Pricing

Cost-plus pricing starts from the company's perspective and works forward to a price. The logic is: what does it cost us to produce this, and what margin do we need to cover our cost of capital and generate acceptable returns?

The mechanics are simple. Total fixed costs divided by projected volume gives you fixed cost per unit. Adding variable cost per unit gives total cost per unit. Adding the target margin gives the floor price — the price below which the company would be better off not producing at all.

This floor is genuinely useful. It tells you the minimum price that makes the business viable. But it is only a floor, not a price. The cost-plus methodology answers "what do we need?" not "what can we get?" A company that prices purely on cost-plus leaves money on the table whenever its value-based price is above its cost-plus floor — which, for a well-differentiated product, is almost always.

The cost-plus methodology also contains a circular logic problem worth understanding. If you increase volume, your fixed cost per unit decreases, which means your cost-plus price decreases. But the appropriate price isn't determined by your volume; it's determined by what the market will pay. You're using a price to determine volume to determine cost to determine price — the calculation chases its own tail unless you anchor it to an external reference.

Where cost-plus pricing is most useful: it anchors the floor, preventing the mistake of pricing below full cost recovery; it informs break-even analysis, as discussed in the previous chapter; and it disciplines cost-intensive businesses against underestimating their true unit costs when volumes are low and fixed cost absorption is incomplete.

### Competitor-Based Pricing

Competitor-based pricing uses the market's existing price structure as the primary reference. The approach is: understand what comparable products charge, understand how your product differs from those comparables, and price to reflect that differential accurately.

The analytical work has three components.

**Competitive benchmarking** establishes the reference prices. What do the closest alternatives charge? How are they packaged — per unit, per subscription, per outcome? What customer segments do they primarily serve? The goal is a clear picture of the price range the market currently accepts and where within that range different products sit.

**Differential analysis** asks what your product does differently from the alternatives. This requires honest assessment of both advantages and disadvantages. A product that is faster but less durable than the competition should be priced to reflect both dimensions: premium for speed, discount for durability shortfall, net position relative to the reference price. The common failure mode here is counting advantages while ignoring disadvantages — which produces a price the market won't accept.

**Market willingness to pay** in the competitive context is the synthesis: given the competitive landscape and your product's differential position, where in the price range do customers place your product? This is ultimately an empirical question, but it can be estimated analytically through differential analysis before empirical data is available.

The limitation of competitor-based pricing used in isolation is that it anchors to the existing market price structure without asking whether that structure correctly reflects value. If the entire market is underpricing — if customers would pay more for any of the available alternatives — competitor-based pricing guides you to underprice too. This is why the three methodologies should be used together, triangulating toward a price grounded in value, covering costs, and positioned sensibly relative to competition.

## Constructing Proxies When Direct Data Isn't Available

One of the skills that distinguishes strong pricing analysis is the ability to construct defensible estimates when ideal data isn't directly observable. In most real pricing situations — and in many case interviews — you won't have direct evidence of willingness to pay or precise opportunity cost data. What you have are partial data points, analogies, and logical inference.

Consider a company launching a new enterprise software product that reduces finance team hours spent on month-end close by 40%. What should they charge?

Willingness to pay isn't directly observable before launch. But you can construct a proxy. The finance team at a mid-size company spends roughly 2,000 hours on month-end close per year. A 40% reduction is 800 hours. At a fully-loaded cost of $80 per hour for a finance professional, that's $64,000 in annual value delivered. A customer who captures all that value would rationally pay up to $64,000 annually. But customers never pay the full value — they need to retain some surplus or there's no reason to switch. At a 50% value split, the price would be $32,000 annually.

This is a rough calculation. But rough calculations grounded in logic are far more useful than either guesses or refusals to estimate. You can stress-test the inputs: what if the relevant labor cost is $60 per hour rather than $80? What if adoption captures only 70% of the theoretical reduction? You get a range, not a point, and the range tells you approximately where the price should sit.

The same logic applies to opportunity cost proxies. If a drug reduces average hospital stay by two days and the average daily hospital cost is $3,000, the value per patient is $6,000. If the drug treats 10,000 patients annually for a hospital system, the total value delivered is $60 million. The drug price should capture some fraction of that — enough to make the pharmaceutical company's investment worthwhile, low enough to leave sufficient surplus for the hospital to justify adoption.

The construction of proxies is part quantitative skill and part judgment about which analogies are appropriate. The discipline is being explicit about assumptions, transparent about uncertainty, and rigorous about the range of plausible outcomes.

## Strategic Objectives Shape the Price

All three methodologies assume a specific strategic objective: maximize value capture at the transaction level. But pricing decisions frequently serve broader strategic objectives that modify the pure value-capture logic.

**Penetration pricing** sets price below the value-based optimum in order to accelerate adoption. The logic is that market share, once captured, has durable value — customers who adopt a product are costly to switch away, and early adoption creates network effects or learning advantages that compound over time. Penetration pricing is appropriate when the long-run value of a customer relationship substantially exceeds the near-term margin being sacrificed.

**Skimming** sets price at the high end of willingness-to-pay initially, extracting maximum value from early adopters, then reduces price over time as competition intensifies and the customer base expands into less price-tolerant segments. This is a common strategy for technology products and pharmaceuticals, where early adopters have high willingness to pay and competitive pressure to reduce prices builds gradually.

**Loss-leader pricing** prices one product below cost in order to drive adoption of a higher-margin companion product. Printers and ink cartridges are the classical example: the printer is priced aggressively, the ink is where the margin lives. The economics of this strategy depend on the attach rate — the fraction of printer buyers who also buy ink — and the margin on the ink relative to the loss on the printer. Get the attach rate wrong and the strategy destroys value rather than creating it.

**Competitive neutralization** prices at parity with a competitor specifically to prevent customers from switching, even when the company's product would command a premium. This is a defensive strategy, appropriate when the competitive threat is severe enough that preserving market share outweighs the margin sacrifice from underpricing.

Each of these strategic objectives implies a different use of the three methodologies. The methodology tells you what the product could reasonably charge. The strategic objective tells you where within that range to actually sit, given what you're trying to accomplish.

## Triangulating to a Price

Strong pricing analysis doesn't pick one methodology and ignore the others. It uses all three as triangulation points and looks for where they converge.

The value-based analysis gives you the ceiling: the maximum price at which customers will buy. The cost-plus analysis gives you the floor: the minimum price at which the business is viable. The competitor-based analysis gives you the anchor: the range the market currently accepts.

If the ceiling is well above the floor and the current market anchor is below the ceiling, you have pricing power — the product can command a premium over competitive alternatives, and the margin generated will substantially exceed cost. This is the ideal situation, and it's where the most interesting pricing work happens: deciding how aggressively to push toward the ceiling, how to segment pricing to capture different parts of the willingness-to-pay distribution, and how to manage the competitive response to premium pricing.

If the ceiling is barely above the floor, the product is a commodity in its market context. Pricing strategy becomes primarily about cost management: reducing the floor through operational efficiency, and defending against competitive pressure that pushes the ceiling down further.

If the ceiling is below the floor — if the market won't pay enough to cover costs — the product is not commercially viable at its current cost structure and current market position. Either costs need to come down, or the product needs to be repositioned to a customer segment with higher willingness to pay, or the launch should not proceed.

This three-point triangulation is the final output of pricing analysis: not a single number, but a range with a recommended position within that range, justified by the intersection of value, cost, and competitive dynamics.

## The Water on the Hiking Trail

Return for a moment to the water bottle at the end of the hike. The grocer charges a dollar because the market for water at point of purchase is competitive, alternatives are plentiful, and cost-plus pricing produces something close to the market price. The hiker would pay ten dollars or twenty dollars or whatever they had in their pocket, because the alternatives have collapsed to zero and the value of hydration in that moment is enormous.

Neither price is wrong. Both prices accurately reflect the relationship between the product and the buyer at the moment of transaction. What changed between them is not the water. What changed is the competitive context, the buyer's alternatives, and the urgency of the need.

This is the underlying truth every pricing framework is trying to capture. Price is not a property of a product. It is a property of a relationship between a product and a market at a specific moment. Change the relationship — change who the buyer is, what alternatives they have, what urgency they feel — and the right price changes with it.

The methodologies in this chapter are tools for analyzing that relationship systematically. Value-based pricing asks what the relationship is worth to the buyer. Cost-plus pricing asks what the relationship needs to return to the seller. Competitor-based pricing asks what comparable relationships currently clear for in the market. Together they triangulate toward the price that accurately reflects the full picture.

Get that picture right and pricing becomes one of the most powerful levers in the business. Get it wrong — either by leaving value on the table or by pricing above what the market will bear — and all the operational excellence in the world won't compensate for the value that was either surrendered or never captured.

That is why pricing matters. And that is why it deserves the careful, triangulated analysis this framework provides.

## A note about AI

Pricing cases reward the candidate who can hold three different pricing logics — cost-plus, competition-based, value-based — in mind at once. The model can recite all three. Holding them simultaneously is the test.

Where the model genuinely helps: running a pricing decision through all three logics on the same case and showing where each logic recommends different action. The triangulation is informative.

Where the model does damage: declaring a final price. Pricing in a case interview is an argument for a position, and the argument has to be yours.

The rule: model triangulates; you commit.
