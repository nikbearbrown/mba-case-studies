# Chapter 8 — Pricing Framework
*The Value Is Not in the Thing.*

Here is a question that sounds simple and isn't: what is something worth?

Not what does it cost to make. Not what does the seller want for it. What is it actually worth — to the specific person buying it, in the specific moment they're deciding whether to buy?

A bottle of water at a grocery store is worth about a dollar. The same bottle of water at the end of a long hike, when you're dehydrated and the nearest store is six miles back, is worth considerably more. The water is identical. The molecules haven't changed. What changed is the buyer's situation — the alternatives available, the urgency of the need, the cost of going without. The value of the thing is not in the thing. It is in the relationship between the thing and the person who needs it.

This is the foundational insight of pricing. Price is not a number attached to a product. Price is the mechanism by which a seller captures a fraction of the value a buyer receives. Set it too low and the seller leaves value on the table — they've transferred wealth to the buyer unnecessarily. Set it too high and the buyer doesn't purchase — the deal that would have benefited both parties doesn't happen. Pricing is the art of finding where in that range the transaction should occur.

The reason pricing cases appear in consulting interviews is that this art is genuinely difficult, genuinely consequential, and genuinely analytical. A one-percentage-point improvement in price, for a company with thin margins, can increase profits by 10 or 15 or 20 percent. No operational efficiency, no cost reduction program, no volume initiative comes close to the leverage that pricing has on the bottom line. Companies routinely leave enormous amounts of money on the table by pricing too low, and occasionally destroy themselves by pricing too high. Getting it right requires exactly the kind of structured analysis that consulting develops.

---

The single most important question in a pricing case is not "what methodology should we use?" It is "what kind of product are we pricing, and what does that tell us about the relevant approach?"

This matters because pricing methodology is not universal. The approach appropriate for a genuinely novel product — something that has never existed before and that customers have no reference point for — is completely different from the approach appropriate for a product that enters an established competitive market where customers have multiple familiar alternatives. Applying the wrong methodology doesn't just produce an imprecise answer; it produces an answer oriented toward the wrong considerations entirely.

Four product categories determine the analytical emphasis, and identifying which category you're in is the first decision in any pricing case.

A new invention is a product that creates a category. Before the iPhone, there was no market for smartphones as we now understand them. Before Airbnb, there was no market for peer-to-peer short-term home rental at scale. When a product creates a category, customers have no existing reference price — they don't know what something like this "should" cost because nothing like this has existed. The methodology that applies is almost entirely value-based: cost and competition provide almost no guidance, so you have to understand what the product is worth to the customer, what problem it solves, what alternatives it replaces, and what those alternatives cost.

A modification to an existing product improves on what already exists — faster, more durable, more accurate, more convenient — without creating a new category. The existing product sets the customer's reference price, and the question becomes how much the improvement is worth above that reference. A drug that reduces hospital stays by two days is worth something calculable if you know what hospital stays cost. A manufacturing machine that increases throughput by 20% is worth something calculable if you know the value of that incremental output. The value-based approach still applies, but now there's an anchor — the cost of the alternative the product improves on.

A product similar to existing offerings faces the most competitive pricing situation. The customer has several alternatives, roughly comparable in function, and price becomes a primary differentiator. Competitor-based pricing dominates here: you must understand what comparable products charge and what your product offers differently, and price to reflect that differential accurately. Pricing too far above comparable products requires a value justification the customer finds credible; pricing too far below signals inferior quality or an unsustainable attempt to buy market share.

Pricing relative to another product in your own portfolio is a different problem altogether. The question is not how to maximize value capture on a single product but how to price the portfolio to maximize overall profitability without cannibalizing your own higher-margin offerings. This requires understanding how a price change on one product affects demand for another, and designing a structure where each tier serves a distinct customer segment without undermining the others.

---

With the product category established, the three pricing methodologies can be applied with appropriate emphasis. Each one asks a different question. Together they triangulate toward a price grounded in reality rather than in any single perspective.

Value-based pricing starts from the customer's perspective and works backward. The core question is what the product is worth to the buyer, and that question breaks into two subsidiary questions that must be answered separately before they can be combined.

The first is willingness to pay: the maximum price at which the customer would purchase rather than go without or seek an alternative. This is not what the customer thinks is "fair," not what they would prefer to pay. It is the threshold above which they say no. Willingness to pay varies across customer segments — the same product may be worth much more to a large enterprise than to a small one, much more to a customer facing urgent need than to one with flexibility. Understanding the distribution of willingness to pay across the customer population is essential for deciding both what price to charge and how to segment pricing across customer types.

The second is opportunity cost: the value of the customer's best alternative. What does the customer use now to accomplish the same goal? What does that alternative cost, in money and in other dimensions — time, effort, quality shortfalls? The difference between what the customer's current alternative costs and what your product costs is the value surplus you're delivering. Willingness to pay is ultimately bounded by this surplus: a rational customer won't pay more for your product than it costs them to use the alternative, unless your product delivers something the alternative genuinely cannot.

In practice, willingness-to-pay and opportunity cost analysis often require estimation rather than direct observation. Customers don't always reveal their true willingness to pay — they have strategic reasons to understate it. And opportunity costs are sometimes hard to quantify because they include non-monetary components like time, inconvenience, or quality risk. The analytical work is in constructing defensible proxies: comparable transactions, economic modeling of customer value, analysis of switching costs.

Supply and demand dynamics constrain value-based pricing from the market side. Even if the product delivers enormous value to customers, a competitive market where multiple suppliers can provide it will drive price toward cost as competition intensifies. Value-based pricing works best when the product has some form of protection against competitive imitation: patent protection, trade secrets, proprietary data, network effects, or switching costs that make competition slow to erode the pricing power.

Cost-plus pricing starts from the company's perspective and works forward. Total fixed costs divided by projected volume gives fixed cost per unit. Adding variable cost per unit gives total cost per unit. Adding the target margin gives the floor price — the minimum below which the company would be better off not producing at all.

This floor is genuinely useful. It tells you the minimum price that makes the business viable. But it is only a floor, not a price. Cost-plus answers "what do we need?" not "what can we get?" A company that prices purely on cost-plus leaves money on the table whenever its value-based price is above its cost-plus floor — which, for a well-differentiated product, is almost always.

The cost-plus methodology also contains a circular logic problem worth understanding. If you increase volume, your fixed cost per unit decreases, which means your cost-plus price decreases. But the right price isn't determined by your volume; it's determined by what the market will pay. The calculation chases its own tail unless you anchor it to an external reference. Where cost-plus pricing is most useful: it anchors the floor, preventing the mistake of pricing below full cost recovery, and it informs break-even analysis for capital-intensive decisions.

Competitor-based pricing uses the market's existing price structure as the primary reference. What do the closest alternatives charge? How are they packaged — per unit, per subscription, per outcome? What customer segments do they primarily serve? The goal is a clear picture of the price range the market currently accepts and where within that range different products sit.

The analytical work has two components beyond that benchmarking. Differential analysis asks what your product does differently from the alternatives — and requires honest assessment of both advantages and disadvantages. A product that is faster but less durable than the competition should be priced to reflect both dimensions: premium for speed, discount for durability shortfall, net position relative to the reference. The common failure mode is counting advantages while ignoring disadvantages, which produces a price the market won't accept.

The limitation of competitor-based pricing used in isolation is that it anchors to the existing market price structure without asking whether that structure correctly reflects value. If the entire market is underpricing, competitor-based pricing guides you to underprice too. This is why the three methodologies should be used together — each one providing a reference point that the others can't supply alone.

---

One of the skills that distinguishes strong pricing analysis is the ability to construct defensible estimates when ideal data isn't directly observable. In most real pricing situations — and in many case interviews — you won't have direct evidence of willingness to pay or precise opportunity cost data. What you have are partial data points, analogies, and logical inference.

Consider a company launching a new enterprise software product that reduces finance team hours spent on month-end close by 40%. A mid-size company's finance team spends roughly 2,000 hours on month-end close per year. A 40% reduction is 800 hours. At a fully-loaded cost of $80 per hour for a finance professional, that's $64,000 in annual value delivered. A customer who captures all that value would rationally pay up to $64,000 annually. But customers never pay the full value — they need to retain some surplus or there's no reason to switch. At a 50% value split, the price would be $32,000 annually.

This is a rough calculation. But rough calculations grounded in logic are far more useful than either guesses or refusals to estimate. You can stress-test the inputs: what if the relevant labor cost is $60 per hour rather than $80? What if adoption captures only 70% of the theoretical reduction? You get a range, not a point, and the range tells you approximately where the price should sit.

The same logic applies to opportunity cost proxies. If a drug reduces average hospital stay by two days and the average daily hospital cost is $3,000, the value per patient is $6,000. If the drug treats 10,000 patients annually for a hospital system, the total value delivered is $60 million. The drug price should capture some fraction of that — enough to make the pharmaceutical company's investment worthwhile, low enough to leave sufficient surplus for the hospital to justify adoption.

The construction of proxies is part quantitative skill and part judgment about which analogies hold. The discipline is being explicit about assumptions, transparent about uncertainty, and rigorous about the range of plausible outcomes. An analyst who can do this confidently — who can build a defensible willingness-to-pay estimate from first principles without direct data — is doing something genuinely useful that the model alone cannot do for them.

---

All three methodologies assume a specific strategic objective: maximize value capture at the transaction level. But pricing decisions frequently serve broader strategic objectives that modify the pure value-capture logic, and a complete pricing analysis has to account for where the company is in its competitive lifecycle.

Penetration pricing sets price below the value-based optimum to accelerate adoption. The logic is that market share, once captured, has durable value — customers who adopt a product are costly to switch away, and early adoption creates network effects or learning advantages that compound over time. Penetration pricing is appropriate when the long-run value of a customer relationship substantially exceeds the near-term margin being sacrificed.

Skimming sets price at the high end of willingness to pay initially, extracting maximum value from early adopters, then reduces price over time as competition intensifies and the customer base expands into less price-tolerant segments. This is common for technology products and pharmaceuticals, where early adopters have high willingness to pay and competitive pressure to reduce prices builds gradually rather than immediately.

Loss-leader pricing prices one product below cost to drive adoption of a higher-margin companion product. Printers and ink cartridges are the classical example: the printer is priced aggressively, the ink is where the margin lives. The economics depend on the attach rate — the fraction of printer buyers who also buy ink — and the margin on the ink relative to the loss on the printer. Get the attach rate wrong and the strategy destroys value rather than creating it.

Competitive neutralization prices at parity with a competitor specifically to prevent customers from switching, even when the company's product would command a premium. This is a defensive strategy, appropriate when the competitive threat is severe enough that preserving market share outweighs the margin sacrifice from underpricing.

Each of these strategic objectives implies a different use of the three methodologies. The methodology tells you what the product could reasonably charge. The strategic objective tells you where within that range to actually sit, given what you're trying to accomplish in the market.

---

Strong pricing analysis doesn't pick one methodology and ignore the others. It uses all three as triangulation points and looks for where they converge.

The value-based analysis gives you the ceiling: the maximum price at which customers will buy. The cost-plus analysis gives you the floor: the minimum price at which the business is viable. The competitor-based analysis gives you the anchor: the range the market currently accepts.

If the ceiling is well above the floor and the current market anchor is below the ceiling, you have pricing power — the product can command a premium over competitive alternatives, and the margin generated will substantially exceed cost. This is where the most interesting pricing work happens: deciding how aggressively to push toward the ceiling, how to segment pricing to capture different parts of the willingness-to-pay distribution, and how to manage the competitive response to premium pricing.

If the ceiling is barely above the floor, the product is a commodity in its market context. Pricing strategy becomes primarily about cost management: reducing the floor through operational efficiency, and defending against competitive pressure that pushes the ceiling down further.

If the ceiling is below the floor — if the market won't pay enough to cover costs — the product is not commercially viable at its current cost structure and current market position. Either costs need to come down, or the product needs repositioning to a customer segment with higher willingness to pay, or the launch should not proceed.

This three-point triangulation is the final output of pricing analysis: not a single number, but a range with a recommended position within that range, justified by the intersection of value, cost, and competitive dynamics.

---

Return for a moment to the water bottle at the end of the hike. The grocer charges a dollar because the market for water at point of purchase is competitive, alternatives are plentiful, and cost-plus pricing produces something close to the market clearing price. The hiker would pay ten dollars or twenty dollars or whatever they had in their pocket, because the alternatives have collapsed to zero and the value of hydration in that moment is enormous.

Neither price is wrong. Both prices accurately reflect the relationship between the product and the buyer at the moment of transaction. What changed between them is not the water. What changed is the competitive context, the buyer's alternatives, and the urgency of the need.

This is the underlying truth every pricing framework is trying to capture. Price is not a property of a product. It is a property of a relationship between a product and a market at a specific moment. Change the relationship — change who the buyer is, what alternatives they have, what urgency they feel — and the right price changes with it.

The methodologies in this chapter are tools for analyzing that relationship systematically. Value-based pricing asks what the relationship is worth to the buyer. Cost-plus pricing asks what the relationship needs to return to the seller. Competitor-based pricing asks what comparable relationships currently clear for in the market. Together they triangulate toward the price that accurately reflects the full picture.

Get that picture right and pricing becomes one of the most powerful levers in the business. Get it wrong — either by leaving value on the table or by pricing above what the market will bear — and all the operational excellence in the world won't compensate for the value that was surrendered or never captured.

## Exercises

**Warm-up**

1. Classify each of the following products into one of the four pricing categories (new invention, modification to an existing product, similar to existing offerings, pricing within a portfolio) and name the primary methodology that should lead the analysis for each: (a) a noise-canceling headphone entering a crowded market, (b) a prescription drug that cuts chemotherapy side effects by 60%, (c) a new streaming service launching alongside Netflix and Disney+, (d) a budget tier added to an existing enterprise software suite. *(Tests: product classification — identifying which analytical lens leads before methodology selection)*

2. A software company's fully-loaded cost per unit is $120 and it targets a 40% gross margin. What is its cost-plus floor price? If its value-based ceiling turns out to be $280 and the closest competitor charges $195, describe in two sentences where within the range you would recommend positioning the price and why. *(Tests: cost-plus floor mechanics and triangulation logic)*

3. A product is priced at $50. The closest alternative costs the customer $45. A candidate argues this means the product is overpriced. What is wrong with this argument? What additional information is needed before concluding anything about the price relative to competition? *(Tests: differential analysis — the failure mode of benchmarking without accounting for the value of the difference)*

**Application**

4. A medical device company has developed a surgical robot that reduces average operating time by 25 minutes per procedure. A hospital performs 4,000 surgeries per year. An operating room costs approximately $36 per minute to run (staff, equipment, overhead). Construct a value-based willingness-to-pay estimate for this device. Then stress-test it: what happens to the estimate if the hospital captures only 60% of the theoretical time savings in practice? *(Tests: proxy construction from first principles — quantifying value delivered, converting to dollars, applying a value-capture fraction, stress-testing)*

5. A consumer electronics company sells a premium printer at $299 and proprietary ink cartridges at $42 each. Customers average 8 cartridge purchases per year. A competitor sells a printer at $149 with compatible cartridges at $18. Assume equal print quality. Calculate the total three-year cost of ownership for each option. At what cartridge price would the premium printer become the better value over three years? What does this tell you about the company's actual pricing strategy? *(Tests: loss-leader pricing mechanics — tracing how the economics of the attached product determine whether the strategy creates or destroys value)*

6. A B2B software company is considering two pricing strategies for its new product: (a) penetration pricing at $8,000 per year to accelerate adoption, or (b) skimming at $22,000 per year to maximize early revenue. The value-based ceiling is $30,000. The cost-plus floor is $5,000. Competitors charge between $12,000 and $18,000. Describe the market conditions under which each strategy makes sense. What is the key variable that would cause you to recommend one over the other? *(Tests: strategic objective selection — connecting competitive lifecycle context to pricing strategy)*

**Synthesis**

7. A pharmaceutical company launches a drug that reduces annual hospitalizations for a chronic condition from 3.2 to 1.1 per patient. The average hospitalization cost is $14,000. The drug treats a patient population of 200,000 in the U.S. Construct a complete triangulation: estimate the value-based ceiling using opportunity cost logic, identify what you would need to establish the cost-plus floor, and describe what competitor-based benchmarking would require given this is a new mechanism of action with no direct therapeutic equivalent. Where do the three analyses converge, and what does that tell you about the appropriate price range? *(Tests: full triangulation — applying all three methodologies to a single case and synthesizing a range)*

8. A company's product is priced at $200. Its cost-plus floor is $190. Its value-based ceiling is $210. Its main competitor charges $185. In two sentences, diagnose the strategic situation. In two more, recommend what the company should actually do — and be specific about whether the answer is a pricing decision or something else entirely. *(Tests: commodity diagnosis — recognizing when the triangulation signals a structural problem that pricing alone can't solve)*

**Challenge**

9. The chapter argues that pricing is not a property of a product but a property of a relationship between a product and a market at a specific moment. Identify a real product or service where this principle creates a genuine ethical tension — where the "correct" price by value-based logic would strike most people as exploitative. Describe the tension without resolving it, then identify what constraint (regulatory, reputational, competitive, or structural) typically prevents markets from clearing at the value-based optimum in these cases. *(Tests: the limits of the framework — where value-based logic is analytically correct but incomplete as a guide to action)*

## A Note About AI

Pricing cases reward the candidate who can hold three different pricing logics — cost-plus, competition-based, value-based — in mind at once. The model can recite all three. Holding them simultaneously is the test.

Where the model genuinely helps: running a pricing decision through all three logics on the same case and showing where each logic recommends different action. The triangulation is informative.

Where the model does damage: declaring a final price. Pricing in a case interview is an argument for a position, and the argument has to be yours.

The rule: model triangulates; you commit.
