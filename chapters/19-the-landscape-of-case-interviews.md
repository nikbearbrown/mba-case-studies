# Chapter 19 — The Landscape of Case Interviews: Formats, Types, and What Each Is Testing

*Why "prepare for case interviews" is like saying "prepare for sports" — the category contains more variation than the name suggests.*

---

Before you can prepare well for something, you need to know what you're preparing for. That sounds obvious. It turns out to be the step most candidates skip.

The phrase "case interview" implies a single thing — a format, a method, a standardized test. It is not. It is a family of related but meaningfully different evaluative approaches, each designed by different firms to measure different capabilities, administered in different modes, and scored against different criteria. A candidate who prepares for the BCG version of a case interview and then walks into a McKinsey interview has prepared for a related but distinct test. A candidate who prepares exclusively for first-round oral cases and then encounters a written case in the final round finds that significant dimensions of their preparation don't apply.

This chapter is a map. It describes the territory of case interview formats — the structural variants, the delivery modes, the problem type categories, the digital assessments, the industry-specific adaptations, and the scoring frameworks — so that the preparation work that follows is directed at the right target. The mechanics of how to perform well in each format are addressed in subsequent chapters. This chapter is about understanding what you're dealing with before you begin.

---

## The Fundamental Split: Who Drives the Case

The most important structural distinction in case interviews is not between firms or between problem types. It is between the two paradigms of control: candidate-led and interviewer-led.

In the candidate-led format, the interviewer presents a business situation and then steps back. The candidate is responsible for the entire trajectory of the analysis — what framework to apply, what data to request, which hypotheses to pursue, which branches to explore and which to deprioritize, and ultimately when and how to conclude. The interviewer functions as a client proxy: present, responsive when asked, but not directing. BCG, Bain, LEK, Deloitte, and PwC primarily use this format. The underlying philosophy is that consulting work requires leadership — the ability to navigate ambiguous problems without continuous supervision.

The characteristic failure mode in candidate-led cases is what practitioners call being "overly framework-driven": the candidate applies a memorized structure mechanically, following its branches in order regardless of what the data is actually saying. This produces analysis that looks organized but isn't responsive to the specific problem. The candidate who excels here operates more like a detective than a student filling in a template — forming a hypothesis early, testing it against the available evidence, and updating the direction of inquiry as each piece of data confirms or disconfirms the hypothesis.

In the interviewer-led format, control over the trajectory belongs to the interviewer. After the candidate provides an initial structure, the interviewer directs the flow through a sequence of pre-determined questions. The candidate doesn't choose which branch to pursue next — the interviewer chooses, and the candidate must respond with depth and precision to wherever the conversation goes. McKinsey is the primary exponent of this format, with Accenture, Strategy&, and Oliver Wyman using variants of it. The design philosophy reflects different priorities: rather than testing independent navigation, the interviewer-led format tests analytical rigor, mental arithmetic speed, and the ability to interpret complex data exhibits accurately under time pressure. Because the interviewer controls the sequence, the format also allows standardized comparison across candidates.

The characteristic failure mode in interviewer-led cases is losing the connection between individual answers and the overall problem. Because the interviewer is directing the sequence, candidates sometimes answer each sub-question in isolation without relating it back to the broader strategic context. The candidate who excels maintains the big picture simultaneously with the specific analysis — answering the immediate question precisely while explicitly connecting it to the client's overall objective.

The structural difference is significant enough that optimal preparation for one is not identical to optimal preparation for the other. Both formats reward structured thinking, quantitative fluency, and clear communication. But candidate-led cases additionally reward the ability to drive a narrative and make good judgments about where to spend analytical attention. Interviewer-led cases additionally reward speed and precision in specific analytical tasks — mental math, chart interpretation, data synthesis — because those tasks will be presented in rapid succession. If you are interviewing primarily at McKinsey, allocate a larger fraction of preparation time to quantitative drills and data exhibit practice. If you are interviewing primarily at BCG or Bain, allocate more to framework construction and hypothesis-driven navigation. If you are interviewing at multiple firms with different formats, calibrate explicitly — don't let preparation for one crowd out preparation for the other.

## How Cases Are Delivered

Independent of who drives the case, cases are administered in several distinct physical and temporal formats, each testing somewhat different dimensions of performance.

The individual oral case is the standard. One candidate, one interviewer, thirty to sixty minutes, real-time dialogue. The oral case tests communication under pressure as much as it tests analytical capability. The candidate must think out loud, narrate their reasoning, respond to interruptions and challenges without losing the thread, and project composure when uncertain. These are not incidental skills — they are central to what consulting actually requires. A consultant who can analyze well but cannot communicate clearly in real time is of limited value to a client who needs to understand and trust the analysis. In final rounds, oral cases are sometimes presented in a "partner case" format where the interviewer is a senior partner. The dynamic shifts: partners are less patient with procedural correctness and more interested in judgment, creativity, and the candidate's ability to engage as a near-peer. The analytical bar is the same; the interpersonal expectation is higher.

A presentation case gives the candidate one to two hours of independent preparation time with a dataset, followed by a structured presentation to a panel. The candidate is expected to identify the key findings, develop a coherent recommendation, and present it in a way that anticipates and addresses likely objections. This format tests the dimensions of analytical work that time pressure prevents an oral case from evaluating: the ability to synthesize a large volume of information, distinguish signal from noise, and structure a recommendation for a senior audience. The panel will probe every assertion. Candidates who have built their recommendation on solid analytical foundations will hold up; candidates who have generated a plausible-sounding narrative from superficial analysis will not. BCG and Bain use presentation cases in certain final-round settings. The appropriate preparation differs from oral case preparation: practice working through a data packet efficiently, identify the three or four most important findings rather than cataloguing everything, and build a presentation structure that leads with the recommendation — the Pyramid Principle applied to a real dataset.

Written cases, used in BCG and Bain final rounds at certain offices, involve working independently with a large data packet — typically twenty to forty pages of charts, tables, and contextual information — and producing written recommendations. This format directly mirrors what junior consultants actually do: extracting insights from messy data, distinguishing the important from the merely interesting, and presenting conclusions in a form that can be reviewed and challenged. Time management is a distinct challenge — the data is deliberately voluminous and the time deliberately limited. The failure mode specific to written cases is over-processing: spending too much time on elaborate analysis of individual data points and running out of time before developing the synthesis and recommendation. The discipline is to read through the entire dataset quickly first, form an initial hypothesis about where the important findings are likely to be, and then do the detailed analysis in the places most likely to yield the key insights.

Group cases present three to six candidates with a shared business problem under the observation of multiple interviewers. The interviewers are not primarily evaluating the quality of the solution — they are evaluating how each candidate behaves in a collaborative problem-solving context. The specific behaviors that signal competence are neither the aggressive dominators nor the passive observers at the extremes. Interviewers look for candidates who contribute substantively, listen genuinely to others' ideas and build on them, correct analytical errors respectfully when they occur, and help move the group toward a conclusion when the discussion is drifting. The failure mode that surprises analytically strong candidates is talking too much. A candidate who contributes excellent individual analysis but consistently interrupts others or drives toward their personal conclusion without building consensus is demonstrating exactly the interpersonal style that would make them difficult to work with on an engagement team.

## Digital Assessments

By 2026, the first evaluative interaction most candidates have with elite firms is not a human-administered interview but a digital or gamified assessment. These tools have become the primary screening mechanism for large application pools, filtering for cognitive capability before the firm invests human interviewer time in candidates.

McKinsey Solve — previously called the Problem Solving Game — is a sixty-to-eighty-minute gamified assessment administered before any human interview. Its design philosophy is distinctive: rather than asking candidates to demonstrate consulting-specific skills directly, it uses game environments to measure the underlying cognitive capabilities that consulting performance depends on. The Redrock Study module places the candidate in the role of a researcher analyzing wildlife population data — wolves, elk, environmental variables — and asks them to investigate data sources, perform numerical calculations, and choose appropriate data visualizations. The Sea Wolf module asks the candidate to select microbes to clean contamination sites based on environmental parameters, testing optimization thinking and pattern recognition.

What makes Solve analytically interesting is its measurement philosophy. It tracks not just the final answers — the Product Score — but the candidate's process: the sequence of actions taken, the time spent in different parts of the problem, the pattern of hypothesis formation and revision — the Process Score. A candidate who reaches correct conclusions through a messy, inefficient process scores differently from one who reaches the same conclusions through a disciplined, systematic process. The game is not just testing whether you can find the right answer but how you go about finding it. Preparation for Solve is different from preparation for oral cases. Practicing case frameworks does not directly improve Solve performance. What helps is practicing numerical estimation under time pressure, working through data interpretation problems quickly and accurately, and developing the habit of systematic hypothesis formation rather than trial-and-error exploration.

BCG uses a chatbot-based assessment called Casey, which delivers one to three structured business cases through sequential multiple-choice, short-answer, and quantitative prompts. A critical design feature: candidates cannot return to previous questions once submitted. Casey tests the candidate's ability to apply business logic quickly and accurately — closer to the oral case format but automated and time-compressed. The inability to revisit previous questions specifically tests forward-directed reasoning: the candidate must commit to each answer and move on, rewarding candidates who form clear hypotheses early and execute decisively rather than those who refine thinking by iterating back through prior answers. Each case ends with a one-way video component where the candidate records a sixty-second structured recommendation — a distinct preparation challenge. Sixty seconds to deliver a structured recommendation to a camera, without an interviewer present, without the social cues that oral cases provide, requires specific practice. The Pyramid Principle — recommendation first, supporting logic second — is the right structure for the sixty-second format. Candidates who bury the recommendation in context-setting will run out of time before the key message is delivered.

## Problem Type Categories

Independent of format and delivery mode, case interview problems fall into recognizable strategic archetypes. Each archetype has a characteristic analytical structure, a dominant framework, and a set of quantitative calculations that typically arise.

Profitability cases are the most foundational. A company's profits are declining, or margins are compressing, or a division is underperforming. The analytical structure decomposes profit into revenue and costs, then decomposes each into its drivers: revenue into price and volume, volume into market size and market share; costs into fixed and variable, then into the value chain stages where costs are concentrated.

Market entry cases ask whether a company should enter a new market or launch a new product. The analytical structure evaluates market attractiveness, the company's capability to compete, the competitive dynamics, and the financial returns of different entry modes. Chapter 6 addresses this type.

Growth cases present a company with a specific growth target and ask how to achieve it. The analytical structure uses the Ansoff Matrix to identify the strategy space and then evaluates specific mechanisms — revenue per user growth, user base expansion, inorganic options — against capability and financial feasibility. Chapter 9 addresses this type.

M&A and due diligence cases ask whether a company should acquire another business, or whether a private equity fund should invest. The analytical structure evaluates standalone value, synergies, acquisition premium, and integration costs, with non-financial cultural and organizational factors as critical complements to the financial analysis.

Pricing cases ask what a company should charge for a product, usually at launch or in response to competitive pressure. The analytical structure triangulates value-based, cost-plus, and competitor-based methodologies.

Market sizing and estimation cases ask the candidate to estimate a quantity without direct data — the number of gas stations in the United States, the daily consumption of a specific product, the size of a market the company is considering entering. These test the ability to construct a logical estimation model from first principles, decompose the problem into estimable components, and arrive at a defensible answer through explicit assumptions rather than guessing. The discipline is showing your work: the process of arriving at the estimate is as important as the estimate itself.

## Industry-Specific Adaptations

The case interview format has been adapted by industries beyond management consulting, each modifying it to test domain-specific capabilities.

Product management cases at technology companies split into product sense questions — "design an app for an amusement park," "how would you improve YouTube?" — and execution questions — "Instagram Reels usage is down 10%, what do you do?" Product sense questions test the ability to identify user needs, prioritize features against those needs, and make product decisions in ambiguous contexts. Execution questions test the ability to define success metrics, build a diagnostic framework for performance problems, and reason from data to root causes. The marketing frameworks in this book apply, but the vocabulary and emphasis differ from consulting cases.

Private equity and investment cases emphasize investment judgment: whether a specific investment will generate returns sufficient to justify the capital at risk. The analytical structure includes standalone company valuation, leverage analysis, and exit scenario modeling. Paper LBO exercises — mental calculations of return on an acquisition using simplified financial assumptions — are a distinct skill requiring specific numerical practice. The approximations worth knowing: a 2.0x return over five years approximates 15% IRR; 2.5x approximates 20%; 3.0x approximates 25%.

Digital transformation cases have become increasingly common as firms advise legacy companies on technology adoption. These cases test the ability to think about technology strategy from a business value perspective — not which technology to implement, but what business problems it would solve, what the adoption risks are, what a minimum viable implementation would look like versus a multi-year transformation, and how to measure whether the investment is generating the promised returns. The failure mode is sounding like a technologist rather than a business advisor: listing features and capabilities rather than reasoning about business value and implementation risk.

## How Cases Are Scored

Understanding how cases are scored is strategically useful because it reveals what evaluators are actually trying to measure — which is not always what candidates assume.

Most firms score candidates across four to five dimensions. Structured thinking, typically weighted at thirty to thirty-five percent, evaluates whether the candidate's approach is MECE — Mutually Exclusive and Collectively Exhaustive — and whether the framework is tailored to the specific problem rather than generically applied. A high score requires demonstrating that the structure was built for this problem, not retrieved from memory and applied regardless of fit.

Quantitative reasoning, weighted at twenty to twenty-five percent, measures mental arithmetic speed and accuracy, the ability to correctly label units and interpret what calculations mean, and the capacity to extract non-obvious insights from data exhibits. The "so what" of a calculation matters as much as its correctness: a candidate who performs the arithmetic accurately but cannot say what the result implies for the client's decision has done half the analytical work.

Business judgment, weighted at fifteen to twenty percent, evaluates whether the candidate demonstrates awareness of real business constraints and trade-offs. Recommendations that are analytically correct but commercially implausible — that require the client to do things no real company would do — signal insufficient business judgment. The test is not whether the recommendation is technically optimal but whether it is executable by an organization with real resource constraints, political dynamics, and risk tolerance.

Communication and presence, weighted at fifteen to twenty percent, evaluates clarity of expression, the ability to state the recommendation before the supporting evidence rather than building to a conclusion, and composure under challenge. The Pyramid Principle — lead with the answer, support with logic — is the communication standard that most firms explicitly teach and evaluate against.

Coachability, sometimes scored separately and sometimes embedded in others, evaluates how the candidate responds when the interviewer introduces new information, challenges an assumption, or hints that the current direction is unproductive. Candidates who update their analysis gracefully when the evidence shifts are demonstrating a capability essential in consulting work. Candidates who defend their initial position against new evidence are demonstrating the opposite.

The scoring scale most firms use runs from one to five. A score of three — average — means the candidate performed acceptably with some guidance and produced a standard but not distinctive framework. A score of four — strong — means consistent logic, minor errors corrected immediately, and clear communication. A score of five — exceptional — means the candidate drove the case independently, generated creative insights that the standard framework would not have produced, and communicated with executive clarity throughout. The score that separates offers from rejections is not five. Most offers go to candidates who consistently score fours with occasional fives. The problem is consistent threes: performing adequately but never demonstrating the capability that would make an interviewer confident in putting the candidate in front of a client.

## The Map and the Territory

This chapter has described the territory. The rest of the book addresses how to navigate it.

The formats are different, the problem types are different, the scoring criteria differ across firms and contexts — but the underlying capability being evaluated is consistent across all of them. Every format, in its own way, is asking: can this person take an ambiguous problem, organize their thinking about it systematically, reason from evidence to conclusions, and communicate those conclusions clearly enough that a client would find them credible and actionable?

The candidate-led case tests that capability through independence. The interviewer-led case tests it through precision. The written case tests it through synthesis. The digital assessment tests it through underlying cognitive process. The group case tests it through collaboration. The PM case tests it through product judgment. The PE case tests it through investment analysis.

Different tests of the same thing. The preparation that builds the underlying capability — rather than the preparation that memorizes responses to specific formats — is what transfers across all of them.

That is what the rest of this book is for.

## A Note About AI

The landscape chapter is meta — it surveys the variation across firms and case types. The model can recite the surface differences and miss the substantive ones.

Where the model genuinely helps: producing a structured comparison of how different firms run their interviews — behavioral mix, case style, evaluation rubric.

Where the model does damage: declaring which firm's style fits you best. The fit assessment is yours and depends on what you actually want from a career.

The rule: comparison from the model; fit from your own honest read.
