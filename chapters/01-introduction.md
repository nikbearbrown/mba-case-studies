## A note about AI

Case interviews are designed to test how you think on your feet. The model thinks fluently and not on its feet. The interview is structurally about a kind of reasoning the model cannot demonstrate to your interviewer.

Where the model genuinely helps: practicing your structure out loud and having the model push back on your assumptions, your numbers, and your logic. The volume of practice matters and the model is patient with reps in a way human coaches are not.

Where the model does damage: producing a finished case answer for you to memorize. Memorized answers fail in interviews — interviewers vary the prompt, and you need to be the one with the framework, not the one with the answer.

The rule: use the model for drills, not for solutions.

## AI Wayback Machine

The ideas in this chapter didn't appear from nowhere. **Marvin Bower** built McKinsey & Company into the prototype management consulting firm in the 1930s — establishing the case-method client engagement and the professional standards that defined the industry. The case interview you're preparing for is downstream of his work.

**Run this:**

```
Who was Marvin Bower, and how does his work building modern McKinsey connect to the case interview we covered in this chapter? Keep it to three paragraphs. End with the single most surprising thing about his career or ideas.
```

→ Search **"Marvin Bower"** on Wikipedia.

**Now make the prompt better.** Try one of these:

- Ask it about Bower's principles for what consulting firms should and shouldn't do.
- Ask it to compare consulting practice in Bower's era with the modern AI-augmented version.

What changes? What gets better? What gets worse?
