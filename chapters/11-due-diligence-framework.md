# Chapter 11 — Due Diligence Framework

In 2012, a private equity firm acquired a healthcare services company for $1.8 billion. The target had strong revenue growth, improving margins, and a defensible position in a fragmented market. The deal team had done thorough financial analysis. The model was clean. The strategic logic was sound.

What the deal team had not adequately investigated was the source of the revenue growth. A significant portion of it came from a billing practice that was, at best, aggressive and, at worst, fraudulent under federal healthcare regulations. Within eighteen months of closing, the company was under investigation by the Department of Justice. The acquirer eventually settled for several hundred million dollars in penalties and disgorgement.

The financial model had been right about the numbers. It had been wrong about what the numbers meant.

This is the fundamental problem that due diligence exists to solve. A financial model tells you what the business has done. Due diligence tells you why it has done it and whether it will continue. The model sees the surface. Due diligence is the attempt to see what's underneath.

---

## What Due Diligence Actually Is

The term "due diligence" is used loosely in business conversation to mean almost any form of pre-decision investigation. In the specific context of private equity and M&A, it has a more precise meaning: the systematic investigation of a target company that a potential acquirer conducts before committing to a transaction.

Due diligence spans multiple workstreams — financial, legal, operational, commercial, environmental, technological — each conducted by specialists examining a specific dimension of the target. The commercial due diligence workstream, which is where consulting firms are most frequently engaged, focuses on the business's market position, competitive dynamics, and growth prospects. It is the workstream that answers the strategic questions: is this business actually as good as the financial statements suggest, and will it continue to be good after we own it?

The due diligence chapter in a case interview context is almost always about commercial due diligence. The question is not whether the financial statements are accurate (that's financial due diligence) or whether the contracts are enforceable (that's legal due diligence). The question is whether the business's commercial position — its market standing, competitive advantage, customer relationships, and growth trajectory — justifies the investment thesis.

Understanding this scope distinction matters for framing your analysis in a case. A candidate who begins a due diligence case by talking about financial statement verification has misread the assignment. The relevant analytical questions are commercial: Is the market attractive? Is this company's position in the market durable? What would have to be true for the investment thesis to be realized?

---

## The Investor's Perspective First

Before the framework, the pre-analysis questions that orient everything else.

**Who is the investor and what are they trying to accomplish?** Private equity funds are not monolithic. A buyout fund acquiring a majority stake to take a company private has different objectives, different time horizons, and different value creation levers than a growth equity fund taking a minority stake in an earlier-stage company. A fund at the beginning of its investment cycle is in a different position than a fund approaching the end of its investment period and under pressure to deploy remaining capital. These contextual factors affect what due diligence questions matter most.

A buyout fund creates value through a combination of financial engineering (using leverage to amplify returns), operational improvement (making the underlying business more profitable), and multiple expansion (selling at a higher EBITDA multiple than the purchase multiple). Due diligence for a buyout needs to evaluate all three levers: can this business support the debt load the financial structure requires, what operational improvements are realistically achievable, and what conditions would support a higher exit multiple in three to five years?

A growth equity fund creates value primarily through revenue growth and margin improvement, with less reliance on leverage. Due diligence for growth equity focuses more heavily on market expansion potential and the management team's ability to capture it.

**What is the investment thesis, specifically?** The investment thesis is the theory of value creation — the story the investment team is telling about why this business will be worth more when they sell it than when they bought it. Every due diligence investigation should be organized around testing that thesis, not around comprehensively examining everything that could possibly be examined.

If the thesis is "this company will benefit from industry consolidation — it will acquire smaller competitors and achieve scale economies" then due diligence must examine whether the acquisition pipeline is real, whether the scale economies are achievable, and whether the management team has the capability to execute a consolidation strategy. If the thesis is "this company has a differentiated product in a growing market and will grow revenue at 25% annually by expanding its sales force" then due diligence must examine whether the product differentiation is genuine and defensible, whether the market is actually growing at the assumed rate, and whether the sales force expansion model works at the economics assumed in the model.

A due diligence that examines everything equally rather than concentrating on the thesis-critical questions is not rigorous — it is diffuse. The constraint on diligence is time and access; using both efficiently requires ruthless prioritization around what would change the investment decision if it turned out to be different from assumed.

## The Three-Phase Structure

Commercial due diligence organizes naturally into three phases: pre-diligence preparation, core analysis, and post-diligence strategic planning. Each phase has distinct objectives and distinct analytical tools.

### Phase One: Pre-Diligence

Pre-diligence is the work done before the investigation begins, to ensure the investigation is correctly oriented. It has three components.

**Investor profile assessment** establishes the context described above — what the fund is trying to accomplish, what their portfolio looks like, what value creation model they apply, and what their historical performance with similar investments has been. A fund that has successfully executed five consolidation plays in fragmented industries has relevant experience that informs both the due diligence process and the post-acquisition value creation plan. A fund making its first investment in a particular sector lacks that experience and needs to compensate with deeper external expertise.

**Historical investment analysis** examines the fund's track record with comparable investments. What did they pay? What did they do post-acquisition? What did they sell for and when? This analysis serves two purposes: it helps assess the fund's credibility as an operator in this sector, and it provides empirical grounding for the assumptions in the current investment thesis. If the fund has previously executed the same thesis — buy at 8x EBITDA, improve margins by 300 basis points over three years, sell at 10x EBITDA — and the historical record shows that thesis realizing at roughly those parameters, the current investment case is better grounded than if the thesis is theoretical.

**Investment objectives clarification** is the process of making the investment thesis explicit and testable. The diligence team should be able to state, before beginning the investigation: "The thesis is X. If we find A, B, and C to be true, the thesis holds. If we find D or E to be true, the thesis breaks." This formulation forces the thesis into a form that the diligence can actually test rather than confirm.

### Phase Two: Core Analysis

Core analysis is the substantive investigation. It has three interconnected components: market evaluation, competitive positioning, and business assessment.

**Market evaluation** asks whether the market the target operates in is genuinely attractive and whether that attractiveness is likely to persist over the investment horizon.

Market size and growth rate are the starting points. But they are not the ending points, and candidates who stop at market size miss most of what matters. A large, fast-growing market that is structurally unattractive — highly competitive, low-margin, commoditized, or subject to rapid disruption — is not a good investment context regardless of its size. The relevant questions are about the forces shaping the market: what drives demand, and is that demand driver durable? What determines competitive intensity, and is the competitive structure stable? What are the key success factors, and are they likely to remain the key success factors?

Market segmentation is often the analytically decisive move in market evaluation. Aggregate market statistics conceal enormous variation across segments. A market that is slow-growing overall may contain fast-growing segments where the target is particularly well-positioned. A market that appears undifferentiated in aggregate may have specific niches where deep expertise creates durable competitive advantage. The question is not "is this market attractive?" but "is the part of this market where this company competes attractive?"

**Competitive positioning** assesses the target's standing within its market: how strong is the position, and how defensible is it?

The analytical framework here is around competitive moats — the structural advantages that protect a company from competitive erosion. Moats can take several forms. Cost advantages allow a company to profitably serve customers at prices competitors cannot sustain. Switching costs make it expensive for customers to move to alternatives, creating retention that doesn't depend on continued performance superiority. Network effects mean the product becomes more valuable as more customers use it, creating a self-reinforcing advantage that is very difficult for new entrants to overcome. Brand and reputation create a customer preference that persists even when product differences are marginal.

The diagnostic question for each potential moat is: how would we know if this moat were actually protecting the business? For switching costs: what is the customer churn rate, and does the historical churn analysis show customer retention that can't be explained by product superiority alone? For network effects: does the value of the product actually increase with scale, and does the data show that larger customer bases produce higher retention and willingness to pay? For brand: does the company command a price premium over comparable unbranded alternatives, and has that premium been stable or growing?

Benchmarking against competitors is essential at this stage. It is not enough to know that the target has strong margins; you need to know whether those margins are strong relative to competitors. A company with 22% EBITDA margins in an industry where 15% is typical is performing excellently. The same company in an industry where 30% is typical is underperforming. The competitive benchmarking tells you whether the target's performance reflects genuine competitive advantage or merely adequate execution in a favorable industry.

**Business assessment** examines the target's operational and financial performance at a level of granularity that the financial statements alone don't provide.

On the financial side, the questions go beyond reported metrics to unit economics: what does the company earn on each customer, each product, each transaction? Are margins consistent across customer segments or concentrated in a subset that might be vulnerable? Is revenue growth driven by new customer acquisition or by expansion within the existing customer base — and which is more durable? Is cash generation tracking reported earnings, or are there working capital dynamics that mean the business is less cash-generative than the income statement suggests?

On the non-financial side, the management team assessment is often decisive. A business with strong fundamentals but weak management will underperform. A business with average fundamentals but exceptional management will frequently outperform. Private equity returns depend critically on management quality because post-acquisition value creation requires execution, and execution depends on people.

Management assessment in due diligence is partly qualitative — observed through interviews, reference checks, and behavioral observation — and partly quantitative, through analysis of operating performance relative to stated plans. Has the management team historically delivered on its forecasts? Where they have missed, was it due to external factors outside their control or internal execution failures? Do they have a track record of successful strategic initiatives, or a pattern of starting initiatives that don't complete?

**Customer analysis** sits within business assessment and deserves specific attention because customer relationships are frequently the most important and most misunderstood asset in a business.

The behavioral segmentation of the customer base — dividing customers by purchase patterns, frequency, lifetime value, and relationship depth — reveals concentrations of risk and opportunity that aggregate metrics hide. A customer revenue base that is 40% concentrated in three customers is structurally fragile in a way that a diversified customer base is not, regardless of how strong the average customer relationship appears. A customer base where the top decile of customers by lifetime value is growing and the bottom decile is churning at high rates is a healthier business than one where the reverse is true.

Psychographic analysis — understanding customer attitudes toward the brand, the perceived switching costs, the loyalty drivers — provides the qualitative substrate that explains the quantitative patterns. A customer base that buys because of genuine product superiority is more durable than one that buys because of inertia or switching costs that are eroding as alternatives improve.

Customer retention rate is perhaps the single most important metric in commercial due diligence. A company that retains 90% of its revenue annually has a fundamentally different risk profile than one that retains 70%. At 90% retention, the company can afford to grow slowly — the base compounds reliably. At 70% retention, the company is running to stand still, continuously replacing churned revenue with new revenue, which requires sustained acquisition investment and is vulnerable to any deterioration in customer acquisition efficiency.

### Phase Three: Post-Diligence Strategic Planning

If the core analysis confirms the investment thesis, post-diligence planning translates the thesis into an operational roadmap.

**Valuation and transaction structuring** uses the insights from core analysis to refine the standalone valuation and quantify the synergies or improvement opportunities that support paying above standalone value. The structuring decisions — how much equity, how much debt, whether to use earn-out provisions that tie part of the purchase price to post-acquisition performance — reflect the diligence findings about risk and uncertainty. Higher uncertainty justifies more earn-out and less upfront payment; more confidence in specific outcomes justifies more upfront payment.

**Growth strategy development** translates the investment thesis into specific initiatives: what will the management team actually do in years one, two, and three to create the value the thesis projects? This requires bridging from the strategic insight — "this company has pricing power it isn't currently utilizing" — to the operational plan — "we will implement a revenue management system and a disciplined pricing review process in the first six months." The gap between strategic insight and operational plan is where most investment theses lose precision, and precision is what makes post-acquisition execution tractable.

**Exit strategy planning** is the investment's endpoint made explicit before commitment. Who are the likely buyers when the fund is ready to sell, in three to five years? What multiple will they pay, and under what conditions? What does the company need to look like at exit to attract those buyers at that multiple? Working backwards from the exit ensures that the value creation plan is oriented toward exit-relevant metrics rather than metrics that look good in management reports but don't translate to buyer interest.

## The Structural Difference Between Due Diligence and M&A Analysis

Due diligence is often treated as a subcomponent of M&A analysis — the investigation phase before the deal closes. In a case interview context, they are related but distinct frameworks, and the difference is worth making explicit.

M&A analysis, as described in the previous chapter, is conducted primarily from the acquirer's perspective. It asks: should we buy this company, at what price, and how will we integrate it? The analytical framework evaluates the deal on the acquirer's terms — synergies with the acquirer's existing business, integration challenges specific to the acquirer's organizational model, strategic fit with the acquirer's portfolio.

Commercial due diligence, by contrast, is conducted from the target company's perspective first. It asks: is this company a good business, independent of who is acquiring it? A company that is an excellent standalone business might be a poor fit for a specific acquirer. A company that is a mediocre standalone business might be an excellent acquisition for an acquirer who can realize specific synergies with it. Understanding the standalone quality of the business is the prerequisite for evaluating deal-specific value creation.

This means that due diligence and M&A analysis use overlapping but not identical frameworks, and a candidate who treats them as equivalent will miss the distinctive analytical contribution of each. The due diligence framework concentrates on the target's intrinsic commercial quality — market position, competitive moats, customer retention, management capability — in ways that the M&A framework addresses only partially. The M&A framework concentrates on synergy quantification and integration planning in ways that due diligence addresses only partially.

## The Healthcare Company, Revisited

Return to the healthcare company at the opening of this chapter. The deal team had done the financial diligence. What they had not done well was the commercial diligence — specifically, the customer analysis and the regulatory risk assessment that would have revealed the fragility of the revenue base.

A rigorous customer analysis would have asked: what specifically is driving the revenue growth, at the level of individual billing codes and service categories? Are the services generating growth the services with the most durable reimbursement structure, or the ones with the most regulatory exposure? Has the growth been continuous, or does it show the pattern of acceleration that is sometimes characteristic of aggressive billing practices?

These questions are not arcane. They are the natural questions of a due diligence team that has internalized the principle of the framework: not "is this business growing?" but "why is it growing, and is the reason for the growth durable?"

The financial model answered the first question. The commercial due diligence was supposed to answer the second. When the second question is not answered, the first answer becomes meaningless — or worse, misleading.

The due diligence framework is ultimately a structure for asking the second question systematically, across every dimension of the business. It doesn't guarantee you'll find every problem. No framework does. What it does is reduce the probability of the failure mode that ends careers and destroys value: mistaking a good-looking surface for a sound structure underneath.

Understanding the surface is easy. Understanding what sustains it is what diligence is for.

## A note about AI

Due diligence is the discipline of finding what would change the answer. The model is biased toward producing answers, not toward finding what would change them.

Where the model genuinely helps: generating the questions a careful diligence team would ask. Question generation is a strength.

Where the model does damage: declaring diligence items satisfied without verification. The model will produce a confident statement that a category has been examined when nothing has been examined.

The rule: model produces questions; you produce answers.
