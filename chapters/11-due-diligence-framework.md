# Chapter 11 — Due Diligence Framework
*The Model Was Right About the Numbers. It Was Wrong About What the Numbers Meant.*

In 2012, a private equity firm acquired a healthcare services company for $1.8 billion. The target had strong revenue growth, improving margins, and a defensible position in a fragmented market. The deal team had done thorough financial analysis. The model was clean. The strategic logic was sound.

What the deal team had not adequately investigated was the source of the revenue growth. A significant portion of it came from a billing practice that was, at best, aggressive and, at worst, fraudulent under federal healthcare regulations. Within eighteen months of closing, the company was under investigation by the Department of Justice. The acquirer eventually settled for several hundred million dollars in penalties and disgorgement.

The financial model had been right about the numbers. It had been wrong about what the numbers meant.

This is the fundamental problem that due diligence exists to solve. A financial model tells you what the business has done. Due diligence tells you why it has done it and whether it will continue. The model sees the surface. Due diligence is the attempt to see what's underneath.

---

The term "due diligence" gets used loosely in business conversation to mean almost any form of pre-decision investigation. In the specific context of private equity and M&A, it has a more precise meaning: the systematic investigation of a target company that a potential acquirer conducts before committing to a transaction.

Due diligence spans multiple workstreams — financial, legal, operational, commercial, environmental, technological — each conducted by specialists examining a specific dimension of the target. The commercial due diligence workstream, where consulting firms are most frequently engaged, focuses on the business's market position, competitive dynamics, and growth prospects. It answers the strategic questions: is this business actually as good as the financial statements suggest, and will it continue to be good after we own it?

This scope distinction matters for framing your analysis in a case. A candidate who begins a due diligence case by talking about financial statement verification has misread the assignment. The relevant questions are commercial: Is the market attractive? Is this company's position in the market durable? What would have to be true for the investment thesis to be realized?

---

Before the framework, two orienting questions that shape everything that follows.

The first is who the investor is and what they're trying to accomplish. Private equity funds are not monolithic. A buyout fund acquiring a majority stake to take a company private has different objectives, different time horizons, and different value creation levers than a growth equity fund taking a minority stake in an earlier-stage company. A buyout fund creates value through a combination of financial engineering, operational improvement, and multiple expansion — buying at one EBITDA multiple and selling at a higher one. Due diligence for a buyout must evaluate all three levers: can this business support the debt load the financial structure requires, what operational improvements are realistically achievable, and what conditions would support a higher exit multiple in three to five years? A growth equity fund creates value primarily through revenue growth and margin improvement, with less reliance on leverage. Due diligence for growth equity concentrates more heavily on market expansion potential and management capability.

The second question is what the investment thesis actually is, stated specifically. The thesis is the theory of value creation — the story the investment team is telling about why this business will be worth more when they sell it than when they bought it. If the thesis is "this company will benefit from industry consolidation — it will acquire smaller competitors and achieve scale economies" then diligence must examine whether the acquisition pipeline is real, whether the scale economies are achievable, and whether the management team can execute a consolidation strategy. If the thesis is "this company has a differentiated product in a growing market and will grow revenue at 25% annually by expanding its sales force" then diligence must examine whether the differentiation is genuine and defensible, whether the market is actually growing at the assumed rate, and whether the sales force expansion model works at the economics assumed in the model.

A due diligence that examines everything equally rather than concentrating on the thesis-critical questions is not rigorous — it is diffuse. The constraint on diligence is time and access; using both efficiently requires ruthless prioritization around what would change the investment decision if it turned out to be different from assumed.

---

Commercial due diligence organizes naturally into three phases: pre-diligence preparation, core analysis, and post-diligence strategic planning. Each has distinct objectives.

Pre-diligence is the work done before the investigation begins, to ensure the investigation is correctly oriented. It means understanding the fund's track record with comparable investments — what did they pay, what did they do post-acquisition, what did they sell for and when — because that history provides empirical grounding for the current thesis. A fund that has previously executed the same thesis and realized it at roughly the assumed parameters is in a different position than a fund advancing a thesis that exists only on paper.

Pre-diligence also means making the investment thesis explicit and testable before beginning. The diligence team should be able to state: "The thesis is X. If we find A, B, and C to be true, the thesis holds. If we find D or E to be true, the thesis breaks." This formulation forces the thesis into a form that the diligence can actually test rather than inadvertently confirm through motivated analysis.

---

Core analysis is the substantive investigation, and it has three interconnected components.

The first is market evaluation. Market size and growth rate are the starting points, but they are not the ending points — candidates who stop there miss most of what matters. A large, fast-growing market that is structurally unattractive — highly competitive, low-margin, commoditized, or subject to rapid disruption — is not a good investment context regardless of its size. The relevant questions are about the forces shaping the market: what drives demand, and is that demand driver durable? What determines competitive intensity, and is the competitive structure stable?

Market segmentation is often the analytically decisive move in market evaluation. Aggregate statistics conceal enormous variation across segments. A market that is slow-growing overall may contain fast-growing segments where the target is particularly well-positioned. A market that appears undifferentiated in aggregate may have specific niches where deep expertise creates durable advantage. The question is not "is this market attractive?" but "is the part of this market where this company competes attractive?"

The second component is competitive positioning — assessing how strong the target's market standing is and, more importantly, how defensible it will remain.

The analytical framework here is around competitive moats: the structural advantages that protect a company from competitive erosion. Cost advantages allow a company to profitably serve customers at prices competitors cannot sustain. Switching costs make it expensive for customers to move to alternatives, creating retention that doesn't depend on continued performance superiority. Network effects mean the product becomes more valuable as more customers use it, creating a self-reinforcing advantage new entrants cannot easily overcome. Brand and reputation create customer preference that persists even when product differences are marginal.

The diagnostic question for each potential moat is: how would we know if this moat were actually protecting the business? For switching costs: what is the customer churn rate, and does the churn analysis show retention that can't be explained by product superiority alone? For network effects: does the data show that larger customer bases produce higher retention and willingness to pay? For brand: does the company command a price premium over comparable alternatives, and has that premium been stable or growing?

Competitive benchmarking is essential at this stage. It is not enough to know that the target has strong margins — you need to know whether those margins are strong relative to competitors. A company with 22% EBITDA margins in an industry where 15% is typical is performing excellently. The same company in an industry where 30% is typical is underperforming. The benchmarking tells you whether performance reflects genuine competitive advantage or merely adequate execution in a favorable industry.

The third component is business assessment — examining the target's operational and financial performance at a level of granularity that financial statements alone don't provide.

On the financial side, the questions go beyond reported metrics to unit economics: what does the company earn on each customer, each product, each transaction? Are margins consistent across customer segments or concentrated in a subset that might be vulnerable? Is revenue growth driven by new customer acquisition or by expansion within the existing base — and which is more durable? Is cash generation tracking reported earnings, or are there working capital dynamics that mean the business is less cash-generative than the income statement suggests?

On the non-financial side, management quality is often decisive. A business with strong fundamentals but weak management will underperform. A business with average fundamentals but exceptional management will frequently outperform. Management assessment in due diligence is partly qualitative — through interviews, reference checks, behavioral observation — and partly quantitative, through analysis of operating performance relative to stated plans. Has the management team historically delivered on its forecasts? Where they've missed, was it due to external factors or internal execution failures? Do they have a track record of successful strategic initiatives, or a pattern of starting initiatives that don't complete?

---

Customer analysis sits within business assessment and deserves particular attention, because customer relationships are frequently the most important and most misunderstood asset in a business.

Behavioral segmentation — dividing customers by purchase patterns, frequency, lifetime value, and relationship depth — reveals concentrations of risk and opportunity that aggregate metrics hide. A customer revenue base that is 40% concentrated in three customers is structurally fragile regardless of how strong the average relationship appears. A customer base where the top decile by lifetime value is growing and the bottom decile is churning at high rates is a healthier business than one where the reverse is true.

Understanding customer attitudes toward the brand — the perceived switching costs, the loyalty drivers, whether customers buy because of genuine product superiority or because of inertia — provides the qualitative substrate that explains the quantitative patterns. A customer base that stays because of genuine preference is more durable than one that stays because alternatives haven't yet matured enough to justify switching.

Customer retention rate is perhaps the single most important metric in commercial due diligence. A company that retains 90% of its revenue annually has a fundamentally different risk profile than one that retains 70%. At 90% retention, the company can afford to grow slowly — the base compounds reliably. At 70%, the company is running to stand still, continuously replacing churned revenue with new revenue, which requires sustained acquisition investment and is vulnerable to any deterioration in acquisition efficiency.

---

If core analysis confirms the investment thesis, post-diligence planning translates it into an operational roadmap.

Valuation and transaction structuring uses the insights from core analysis to refine the standalone valuation and quantify the improvement opportunities that support paying above standalone value. The structuring decisions — how much equity, how much debt, whether to use earn-out provisions that tie part of the purchase price to post-acquisition performance — reflect the diligence findings about risk and uncertainty. Higher uncertainty justifies more earn-out and less upfront payment.

Growth strategy development translates the investment thesis into specific initiatives: what will the management team actually do in years one, two, and three to create the value the thesis projects? The gap between strategic insight and operational plan is where most investment theses lose precision. "This company has pricing power it isn't currently utilizing" is a strategic insight. "We will implement a revenue management system and a disciplined pricing review process in the first six months" is an operational plan. Precision is what makes post-acquisition execution tractable.

Exit strategy planning makes the investment's endpoint explicit before commitment. Who are the likely buyers in three to five years? What multiple will they pay, and under what conditions? What does the company need to look like at exit to attract those buyers at that multiple? Working backward from the exit ensures the value creation plan is oriented toward exit-relevant metrics rather than metrics that look good internally but don't translate to buyer interest.

---

Due diligence and M&A analysis are related but distinct frameworks, and the difference matters for case interviews.

M&A analysis is conducted primarily from the acquirer's perspective. It asks: should we buy this company, at what price, and how will we integrate it? The analytical framework evaluates the deal on the acquirer's terms — synergies with the acquirer's existing business, integration challenges specific to the acquirer's organization, strategic fit with the acquirer's portfolio.

Commercial due diligence is conducted from the target company's perspective first. It asks: is this company a good business, independent of who is acquiring it? A company that is an excellent standalone business might be a poor fit for a specific acquirer. A company that is a mediocre standalone business might be an excellent acquisition for an acquirer who can realize specific synergies with it. Understanding the standalone quality of the business is the prerequisite for evaluating deal-specific value creation.

A candidate who treats these frameworks as equivalent will miss the distinctive analytical contribution of each. Due diligence concentrates on the target's intrinsic commercial quality — market position, competitive moats, customer retention, management capability — in ways that M&A analysis addresses only partially. M&A analysis concentrates on synergy quantification and integration planning in ways that due diligence addresses only partially. Both are required; neither substitutes for the other.

---

Return to the healthcare company at the opening. The deal team had done the financial diligence. What they had not done well was the commercial diligence — specifically, the customer analysis and the regulatory risk assessment that would have revealed the fragility of the revenue base.

A rigorous customer analysis would have asked: what specifically is driving the revenue growth, at the level of individual billing codes and service categories? Are the services generating growth the ones with the most durable reimbursement structure, or the ones with the most regulatory exposure? Does the growth pattern show the continuous trajectory of a business serving real and expanding demand, or the acceleration pattern sometimes characteristic of aggressive billing practices?

These questions are not arcane. They are the natural questions of a diligence team that has internalized the frame of the framework: not "is this business growing?" but "why is it growing, and is the reason for the growth durable?"

The financial model answered the first question. The commercial due diligence was supposed to answer the second. When the second question is not answered, the first answer becomes meaningless — or worse, misleading.

The due diligence framework is ultimately a structure for asking the second question systematically, across every dimension of the business. It doesn't guarantee you'll find every problem. No framework does. What it does is reduce the probability of the failure mode that ends careers and destroys value: mistaking a good-looking surface for a sound structure underneath.

Understanding the surface is easy. Understanding what sustains it is what diligence is for.

## Exercises

**Warm-up**

1. A private equity fund is evaluating two targets in the healthcare software market. Target A has 92% annual revenue retention and 18% EBITDA margins in an industry where 12% is typical. Target B has 71% annual revenue retention and 26% EBITDA margins. Which target has the stronger commercial profile, and why? What additional information would you want before concluding? *(Tests: retention rate as a risk signal — recognizing that a high margin can mask a structurally fragile business)*

2. A buyout fund's investment thesis is: "We will acquire this regional logistics company, expand it into two adjacent markets over three years, and sell it to a strategic buyer at a premium multiple." Restate this thesis in the falsifiable form the chapter recommends: identify three findings that would confirm it and two findings that would break it. *(Tests: thesis formulation — converting a narrative into a testable structure before diligence begins)*

3. A target company's aggregate revenue has grown 18% annually for three years. The diligence team segments the customer base and finds: top 10 customers (40% of revenue) have grown 31% annually; customers ranked 11–50 have grown 6% annually; customers below rank 50 have declined 4% annually. What does this pattern suggest about the quality of the revenue growth, and what questions does it raise? *(Tests: segmentation as the decisive move — how aggregate statistics conceal concentrated risk)*

**Application**

4. A company claims it has strong brand moat. Its gross margins are 48% in a market where comparable unbranded alternatives sell at 39% gross margins. Over the past four years, this premium has moved from 11 percentage points to 9 percentage points. Assess whether the moat evidence is strong, weakening, or inconclusive. What one additional piece of data would most change your assessment? *(Tests: moat diagnostics — using the right data to distinguish genuine from claimed competitive advantage)*

5. A growth equity fund is evaluating a B2B SaaS company under two investment theses: (a) the company will expand into enterprise customers, tripling average contract value, or (b) the company will expand geographically into Europe, growing its addressable market by 60%. For each thesis, identify the two or three most critical diligence questions — the ones where the answer would change the investment decision — and explain why those questions are decisive for that specific thesis. *(Tests: thesis-driven prioritization — different theses require different diligence concentrations)*

6. A target company's management team has missed its revenue forecast in three of the past four years. In two of those misses, they cited industry headwinds that were real and affected competitors similarly. In one miss, they cited a failed product launch that post-mortems attribute to poor market research. Assess the management team based on this evidence. What is the most important additional piece of information you would want? *(Tests: management assessment — distinguishing external-driven misses from execution-driven misses)*

**Synthesis**

7. A private equity fund has acquired five companies using the thesis "acquire a market leader in a fragmented B2B service industry, consolidate smaller competitors, achieve scale economies." Three of the five have returned 2.8x–3.4x invested capital. Two have returned below 1.0x. Using the due diligence framework, generate three hypotheses for why the failed investments differed from the successful ones. For each hypothesis, identify the diligence question that, if asked rigorously beforehand, would have surfaced the risk. *(Tests: retrospective diagnosis — using the framework to explain outcome variation and identify what better diligence would have found)*

8. You are conducting commercial due diligence on a healthcare services company with strong revenue growth. The growth has accelerated in the past eighteen months, concentrated in two service lines. Reimbursement rates in those service lines have been stable. Referral volume has increased significantly. Using the healthcare acquisition case from the chapter as a template, write the five most important questions a rigorous diligence team would ask — questions specifically designed to distinguish legitimate demand growth from billing practice risk. *(Tests: direct application of the chapter's central case — translating the failure mode into a proactive question set)*

**Challenge**

9. The chapter argues that commercial due diligence should be conducted from the target company's perspective first — asking "is this a good business?" before asking "is this a good acquisition for us?" Describe a realistic scenario where this sequencing leads a diligence team to recommend passing on a deal that would actually have been highly value-creating for a specific acquirer. What does this reveal about the limitation of the standalone-quality-first approach, and how would you modify the framework to account for it? *(Tests: the framework's own blind spot — where acquirer-specific synergies make a mediocre standalone business a superior deal)*

## A Note About AI

Due diligence is the discipline of finding what would change the answer. The model is biased toward producing answers, not toward finding what would change them.

Where the model genuinely helps: generating the questions a careful diligence team would ask. Question generation is a strength.

Where the model does damage: declaring diligence items satisfied without verification. The model will produce a confident statement that a category has been examined when nothing has been examined.

The rule: model produces questions; you produce answers.
