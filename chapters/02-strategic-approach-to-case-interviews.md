# Chapter 2 — Strategic Approach to Case Interviews

*What the room is actually testing, and why most candidates answer the wrong question.*

---

Here is something nobody tells you about case interviews, and it matters: the interviewer is not trying to trick you.

This sounds like the kind of reassurance that precedes a trick. It isn't. The point is more interesting than that. The case interview is not a gotcha exercise. It is, at its core, a simulation — a compressed, artificial version of what actually happens on a consulting engagement. The interviewer is playing the role of a client who has a problem, and you are playing the role of an analyst who has just been handed the project. The interviewer wants you to succeed roughly as much as a real client would want their consultant to succeed. The adversarial framing that most candidates bring to the room is a self-imposed disadvantage.

Understanding this changes everything about how you approach the thing.

---

There is a paradox at the center of case interview preparation. The people who prepare most intensively — who have memorized every framework, who can recite the profitability tree in their sleep, who have done sixty practice cases — sometimes perform worse than people who have done twenty. Not always, and not on average. But the failure mode of over-preparation is real, and it is worth understanding.

Here is what happens. A candidate drills frameworks until the frameworks become reflexive. They hear "profits are declining" and the profitability framework activates automatically: revenues down or costs up, if revenues, then price or volume, if volume, then market size or market share. They are off and running before the problem has been fully described. The framework is doing the thinking, and the candidate is along for the ride.

This feels like preparation. It is actually a form of intellectual laziness dressed up as rigor.

The problem is that frameworks are not solutions. They are search tools. They help you figure out which part of the problem space to look in. But they only help if you have first understood what problem space you are actually in — and that requires something frameworks cannot give you, which is judgment about what this specific situation actually is.

What follows is not a framework either. It is a discipline. A set of habits that keep you oriented toward the actual problem rather than toward the performance of problem-solving. The distinction is subtle but it is everything the interviewer is watching for.

## Actually Understanding the Problem

Most candidates fail to actually start at the beginning.

You are given a problem statement. It might be: "Our client is a European airline that has seen its operating margins compress by four percentage points over the last two years. What would you recommend?" The brain wants to jump immediately to cost structure analysis, fuel prices, load factors, competitive dynamics. Resist this.

Before any of that, ask yourself: do I actually understand what I have been asked?

Operating margins compressed — compressed relative to what? To the client's own history, or to competitors? Four percentage points sounds like a lot for a low-margin business and not much for a high-margin one. Airlines typically run on thin margins; four points might be existential. The last two years — is this a gradual slide or a sudden drop? And the word "recommend" — recommend what, exactly? A diagnosis? A fix? Both?

These are not pedantic questions. They are the questions that determine whether the next twenty minutes of analysis are relevant or irrelevant. Clarifying them takes ninety seconds. Failing to clarify them can cost you the case.

The skill of asking good clarifying questions is not the skill of asking many questions. It is the skill of asking the questions whose answers would change your approach. Here is a useful test: before you ask a question, ask yourself, "If the answer is X, will I do something different than if the answer is Y?" If the answer is no — if you would proceed the same way regardless — do not ask the question. It wastes the interviewer's time and signals that you are stalling rather than probing.

If the answer is yes, ask it. And listen carefully when the interviewer responds, because often the answer to one question opens up a more important question hiding behind it.

There is one more thing about this opening phase that is underappreciated: the interviewer is often withholding information deliberately. Not to trick you — remember, they are not trying to trick you — but because a real client rarely hands you everything relevant in the first five minutes. The client does not always know what is relevant. Part of your job is to figure out what you need to know and ask for it. Interviewers are watching to see whether you can do this. The candidates who never ask for additional information are signaling that they either do not know what they need, or do not know that they do not know, which is worse.

## Building a Structure Worth Having

Structuring is the phase that gets the most attention in case prep, probably because it is the most visible. You write something on the paper in front of you, you walk the interviewer through it, and they can immediately see whether it is coherent. It feels like the exam question with a clearly right or wrong answer.

It is not quite that, and treating it as such produces structures that look right but are not.

A structure worth having does one thing well: it converts an ambiguous situation into a set of navigable questions. Not a comprehensive list of everything that might possibly be relevant — that is a brainstorm, not a structure. A structure is a prioritized, organized set of the questions that are most likely to be decisive.

Consider the airline case. A comprehensive list of potentially relevant factors includes fuel costs, labor costs, maintenance costs, airport fees, ticket prices, load factors, route mix, competitive dynamics, fleet age, regulatory changes, currency exposure, loyalty program performance, ancillary revenue, cabin class mix, and approximately forty other things. You could spend a week on this list and still not have analyzed anything.

A good structure says: we need to understand whether this is primarily a revenue problem or a cost problem, and we need to understand whether it is specific to this airline or shared across the industry. Those two questions will tell us where to look. If it is a revenue problem unique to this airline, we look at their pricing, routes, and customer retention. If it is an industry-wide cost problem, we look at input costs that affect everyone. Get those two questions answered and the structure almost writes itself.

This is the move: instead of listing everything that could possibly be relevant, ask what the two or three questions that would most efficiently narrow the problem space are. That is your structure.

The practical execution matters too. Write it down — not in your head, on the paper in front of you. Speaking a structure before writing it almost always produces a messier structure than writing it first, because writing forces clarity that speaking lets you evade. When you do walk the interviewer through it, be explicit about the logic: "I want to start here because if the answer is X, it tells us Y and we can rule out Z." That kind of narrated reasoning is far more impressive than simply announcing categories.

One more thing: structure is allowed to evolve. If you learn something in the analysis phase that makes your initial structure look misguided, update the structure. Announce the update. "Based on what we just found, I think the more interesting question is actually over here — can I redirect?" Adapting to new information is not a sign of weakness. It is a sign of thinking. Rigidly adhering to a structure that no longer fits the problem is the actual failure mode.

## Analysis as Hypothesis Testing

The analysis phase has a specific character that distinguishes good case analysis from competent-looking-but-useless analysis. Good analysis is hypothesis-driven. It proceeds by forming specific, testable propositions about what is causing the problem, and then looking for evidence that confirms or disconfirms them.

The alternative is data-gathering-driven analysis, which proceeds by collecting all available information and hoping a pattern emerges. This is not analysis. It is archaeology. And it takes too long, produces too much material to sort through, and often misses the actual answer because the actual answer is three levels below the surface and you never got there.

Here is what hypothesis-driven analysis looks like in practice. You have established that the airline's problem is a revenue problem, not a cost problem, and that it is specific to this airline rather than industry-wide. You form a hypothesis: the most likely explanation is that they have lost market share on high-value routes to a low-cost competitor. Now you test it. What does market share data show on the routes where they have seen the biggest revenue declines? Has a specific competitor entered those routes? What has happened to ticket prices on those routes? What has happened to load factors?

Each question is pointed. Each answer either supports the hypothesis, contradicts it, or — most usefully — refines it into a more specific version. "We are losing share on business routes because a competitor is undercutting us on price" is a different problem than "we are losing share because our reliability has declined and business travelers have switched." Both might fit the initial data, but they call for completely different responses. The hypothesis-testing approach gets you to that distinction efficiently.

<!-- → [CHART: Branching decision tree showing how one hypothesis ("revenue problem, company-specific") forks into sub-hypotheses based on data answers — pricing pressure, reliability decline, route abandonment. Illustrates that analysis is a directed search, not an open-ended survey.] -->

The comparison habit belongs here too. Every number needs a reference point to be informative. A load factor of 79% means nothing by itself. A load factor of 79% against an industry average of 85% and a company benchmark of 83% two years ago means a great deal. When analyzing data, instinctively ask: is this number big or small? Compared to what baseline? In which direction has it moved? What rate of change is normal, and what is alarming?

And through all of this: keep talking. Not continuously, not performatively, but enough that the interviewer can follow your reasoning and intervene if you are heading somewhere unproductive. Narrate the hypothesis you are testing and why. Flag when you are surprised by a number. Signal when a dead end has caused you to revise. The analysis phase is not a silent exam. It is a conversation in which you are demonstrating a process, not just producing a result.

## When You Get Stuck

You will get stuck. Every candidate gets stuck. What separates the candidates who recover from the ones who do not is almost entirely about how they handle the moment of being stuck.

The wrong response is to go quiet, stare at the paper, and hope the answer materializes. It rarely does, and the silence communicates anxiety rather than thought.

The right response is to be explicit about where you are and what you are trying to do. "I am not immediately seeing what is driving the volume decline — let me think about what data would help me figure that out." This is not an admission of failure. It is a demonstration that you know you do not know something and you have a plan for figuring it out. That is actually a sophisticated response.

Interviewers frequently provide hints when candidates get stuck. The hints are often subtle — a pointed question, a specific number offered without being requested, an emphasis on a detail mentioned earlier. Stay alert to these. The interviewer is not cheating for you; they are giving you the nudge that a manager would give a junior analyst who needed it. Taking the hint and running with it is the right thing to do. Ignoring it and persisting in the wrong direction is not persistence — it is stubbornness.

There is also value in returning to your structure when you are stuck. The structure is a map. If you have lost your way, look at the map. Which branches have you covered? Which have not you explored? Very often, getting stuck in one branch is a signal that you have already implicitly answered it and need to move to the next one. The structure tells you where that is.

## Recommendations That Actually Recommend Something

The recommendation is the deliverable. Everything before it was preparation. And yet the recommendation is the phase where the most candidates produce the least.

The failure has a specific anatomy. The candidate has done solid analysis. They have identified the problem, traced it to a root cause, and have a reasonably clear sense of what could be done about it. Then, at the moment of synthesis, something happens. The candidate shifts from analyst mode into hedge mode. The recommendation becomes: "There are several options. Option A has advantages X and Y but disadvantages Z and W. Option B would address Z but creates new challenges around V. The company should consider its strategic objectives and risk tolerance in making this decision."

This is not a recommendation. This is a refusal to recommend, dressed up as rigor.

The reason candidates do this is understandable. Committing to a recommendation feels risky. What if it is wrong? What if the interviewer disagrees? What if there are factors you have not considered? The hedged answer feels safer because it is technically defensible — you have not said anything false.

But consulting is not a business that rewards technically defensible non-answers. Clients hire consultants because they need someone to actually take a position — to say "do this" with enough specificity and supporting logic that the client can act on it. The interviewer knows this. When they watch a candidate produce a hedged non-recommendation, they are not impressed by the candidate's careful acknowledgment of uncertainty. They are noting that the candidate cannot do the fundamental thing.

A good recommendation has three properties. It is specific: not "improve customer experience" but "reduce boarding time on domestic routes by implementing a new gate assignment system, targeting a 15% reduction in the next six months." It is prioritized: it identifies the most important action, not a comprehensive list of everything that might help. And it is grounded: it connects directly back to the analysis, so the logic runs cleanly from problem to cause to response.

Apply a sanity check before you deliver it. Does the math hold up? Is the recommendation actually implementable given what you know about the client's resources? Does it address the right version of the problem? The sanity check is not about introducing doubt — it is about ensuring you are not recommending something that falls apart on first examination.

Then deliver it clearly, without excessive hedging, with explicit acknowledgment of the key risk. "We recommend X because of Y and Z. The main risk is W, which we would address by doing V." One sentence on risk is appropriate. Three paragraphs on everything that might go wrong is not a recommendation — it is anxiety in paragraph form.

## The Approach as Character, Not Technique

The four phases — understand, structure, analyze, recommend — are mechanical at the level of description. In practice, executing them well is not mainly a technical challenge. It is mainly a challenge of character.

The candidate who pauses genuinely at the beginning, who asks for what they need and waits for the answer, is demonstrating patience. The candidate who builds a structure and says honestly when it needs updating is demonstrating intellectual honesty. The candidate who narrates their reasoning throughout is demonstrating that they think of analysis as communication, not just computation. The candidate who commits to a recommendation at the end, knowing it might be wrong, is demonstrating the kind of judgment under uncertainty that consultants need every day.

None of these are things you can fake convincingly for forty-five minutes. The interviewers have done this hundreds of times. They know what genuine intellectual engagement looks like versus performance of intellectual engagement. The preparation that actually helps is not the kind that teaches you to perform confidence — it is the kind that builds the real thing, by doing enough cases that the discipline becomes second nature and the anxiety has somewhere to go.

That is what the approach is actually for. Not to produce the right answer, but to produce the kind of thinking that tends to reach good answers, even in situations you have not seen before. The interviewer is not hoping to hand you a familiar problem. They are hoping to watch you think your way through an unfamiliar one.

Show them that.

## Exercises

### Warm-Up

**1.** A case prompt reads: "Our client, a regional grocery chain, has seen net income fall 12% over the past year." Before doing any analysis, write down three clarifying questions you would ask. For each, state what you would do differently depending on the answer. *(Tests: distinguishing decisive from decorative clarifying questions.)*

**2.** You are given the following numbers about the grocery chain: revenue is up 4%, gross margin is flat, and net income is down 12%. Without any additional data, what does this pattern tell you about where the problem is most likely located? What does it rule out? *(Tests: using numbers relationally rather than in isolation.)*

**3.** A candidate's structure for the grocery case lists: pricing, promotions, product mix, store layout, labor costs, supply chain, inventory management, and customer loyalty. Identify the problem with this structure and rewrite it as the two or three questions that would most efficiently narrow the problem space. *(Tests: structure as narrowing versus structure as listing.)*

---

### Application

**4.** For the airline case used throughout this chapter, suppose the data comes back: load factors are flat, ticket prices are down 8% year-over-year, and a low-cost competitor entered three of the airline's top-ten routes eighteen months ago. Formulate the next hypothesis you would test and the specific data you would ask for to test it. *(Tests: hypothesis refinement from data — moving from the general to the specific.)*

**5.** You are twenty minutes into a case when you realize your initial structure assumed a cost problem, but the data clearly points to a revenue problem. Write out what you would say to the interviewer to redirect. Your answer should acknowledge the update, explain why you are making it, and state the new direction — in four sentences or fewer. *(Tests: updating structure under new information without losing credibility.)*

**6.** A candidate ends their case with: "So overall, I think the company should consider a combination of cost reduction and revenue growth strategies, balanced against their risk tolerance and competitive position." Rewrite this as a specific, grounded recommendation using the same hypothetical case data you have been working with. *(Tests: transforming hedge language into a genuine recommendation.)*

**7.** You ask a clarifying question: "Is this margin compression happening industry-wide, or is it specific to our client?" The interviewer says: "Good question — it appears to be specific to our client." What does this tell you, and what does it let you rule out? How does it change where you focus? *(Tests: using clarification answers to actively direct the analysis.)*

---

### Synthesis

**8.** A candidate has done the following: asked two decisive clarifying questions, built a structure that narrows the problem to revenue vs. cost and company-specific vs. industry-wide, found through analysis that the problem is company-specific and revenue-driven, and traced it to price erosion on business routes after a competitor's entry. They now need to deliver a recommendation. Write the recommendation in four to six sentences: specific action, supporting logic, primary risk, and how you would address the risk. *(Tests: integrating all four phases into a coherent output.)*

**9.** You are stuck. You have identified that the problem is revenue and company-specific, but you cannot figure out whether the root cause is pricing, reliability, or route coverage. The interviewer offers no hint. Describe in writing what you would do next — not a list of options, but a concrete next step — and explain why that step would help you break the impasse. *(Tests: productive recovery from stuckness using the structure as a map.)*

### Challenge

**10.** The "AI Wayback Machine" section in this chapter connects case interview strategy to Igor Ansoff's 1965 framework. Run the suggested prompt with an AI model. Then assess the response against the standard this chapter sets for analysis: Is the model distinguishing between hypothesis-driven thinking and data-gathering? Is it forming testable propositions or listing possibilities? Write a one-paragraph evaluation of where the model's strategic thinking holds up and where it defaults to the performance of analysis rather than the thing itself.

**11.** Design a two-question clarifying sequence — where the answer to the first question determines what the second question should be — for this prompt: "Our client is a mid-sized law firm that has seen partner billings decline for the third consecutive year. What is going on?" Your sequence should branch: write the follow-up question for each possible answer to the first. Then explain what analytical direction each branch points toward. *(Tests: clarification as a directed search, not a checklist.)*

## A Note About AI

The strategic approach is the move that separates a candidate who has practiced from one who has thought. The model has practiced. It has not thought, in the sense the interviewer means.

Where the model genuinely helps: stress-testing your strategic approach by playing devil's advocate against your framing. Models surface the obvious objections cleanly.

Where the model does damage: producing your strategic approach for you. Strategy in a case is a function of the case's specifics, and your read of those specifics is the read being graded.

The rule: model objects, you frame.
