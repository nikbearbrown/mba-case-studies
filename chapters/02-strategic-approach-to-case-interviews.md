# Chapter 2 — Strategic Approach to Case Interviews

Here is something nobody tells you about case interviews, and it matters: the interviewer is not trying to trick you.

This sounds like the kind of reassurance that precedes a trick. It isn't. The point is more interesting than that. The case interview is not a gotcha exercise. It is, at its core, a simulation — a compressed, artificial version of what actually happens on a consulting engagement. The interviewer is playing the role of a client who has a problem, and you are playing the role of an analyst who has just been handed the project. The interviewer wants you to succeed roughly as much as a real client would want their consultant to succeed. The adversarial framing that most candidates bring to the room is a self-imposed disadvantage.

Understanding this changes everything about how you approach the thing.

---

## The Problem with Knowing Too Much

There is a paradox at the center of case interview preparation. The people who prepare most intensively — who have memorized every framework, who can recite the profitability tree in their sleep, who have done sixty practice cases — sometimes perform worse than people who have done twenty. Not always, and not on average. But the failure mode of over-preparation is real, and it is worth understanding.

Here is what happens. A candidate drills frameworks until the frameworks become reflexive. They hear "profits are declining" and the profitability framework activates automatically: revenues down or costs up, if revenues, then price or volume, if volume, then market size or market share. They are off and running before the problem has been fully described. The framework is doing the thinking, and the candidate is along for the ride.

This feels like preparation. It is actually a form of intellectual laziness dressed up as rigor.

The problem is that frameworks are not solutions. They are search tools. They help you figure out which part of the problem space to look in. But they only help if you've first understood what problem space you're actually in — and that requires something frameworks can't give you, which is judgment about what this specific situation actually is.

The strategic approach this chapter is about is not a framework. It is a discipline. It is a set of habits that keep you oriented toward the actual problem rather than toward the performance of problem-solving.

## Phase One: Actually Understanding the Problem

Let's start at the beginning, which is where most candidates fail to actually start.

You are given a problem statement. It might be: "Our client is a European airline that has seen its operating margins compress by four percentage points over the last two years. What would you recommend?" Your brain wants to jump immediately to cost structure analysis, fuel prices, load factors, competitive dynamics. Resist this.

Before any of that, ask yourself: do I actually understand what I've been asked?

Operating margins compressed — compressed relative to what? To the client's own history, or to competitors? Four percentage points sounds like a lot for a low-margin business and not much for a high-margin one. Airlines typically run on thin margins; four points might be existential. The last two years — is this a gradual slide or a sudden drop? And the word "recommend" — recommend what, exactly? A diagnosis? A fix? Both?

These are not pedantic questions. They are the questions that determine whether you spend the next twenty minutes doing relevant analysis or irrelevant analysis. Clarifying them takes ninety seconds. Failing to clarify them can cost you the case.

The skill of asking good clarifying questions is not the skill of asking many questions. It is the skill of asking the questions whose answers would change your approach. Here is a useful test: before you ask a question, ask yourself, "If the answer is X, will I do something different than if the answer is Y?" If the answer is no — if you'd proceed the same way regardless — don't ask the question. It wastes the interviewer's time and signals that you're stalling rather than probing.

If the answer is yes, ask it. And listen carefully when the interviewer responds, because often the answer to one question opens up a more important question hiding behind it.

There is one more thing about phase one that is underappreciated: the interviewer is often withholding information deliberately. Not to trick you — remember, they are not trying to trick you — but because a real client rarely hands you everything relevant in the first five minutes. The client doesn't always know what's relevant. Part of your job is to figure out what you need to know and ask for it. Interviewers are watching to see whether you can do this. The candidates who never ask for additional information are signaling that they either don't know what they need or don't know they don't know, which is worse.

## Phase Two: Building a Structure Worth Having

Structuring is the phase that gets the most attention in case prep, probably because it's the most visible. You write something on your sheet of paper, you walk the interviewer through it, and they can immediately see whether it's coherent. It feels like the exam question with a clearly right or wrong answer.

It is not quite that, and treating it as such produces structures that look right but aren't.

A structure worth having does one thing well: it converts an ambiguous situation into a set of navigable questions. Not a comprehensive list of everything that might possibly be relevant — that's a brainstorm, not a structure. A structure is a prioritized, organized set of the questions that are most likely to be decisive.

This distinction matters. Consider the airline case again. A comprehensive list of potentially relevant factors includes fuel costs, labor costs, maintenance costs, airport fees, ticket prices, load factors, route mix, competitive dynamics, fleet age, regulatory changes, currency exposure, loyalty program performance, ancillary revenue, cabin class mix, and approximately forty other things. You could spend a week on this list and still not have analyzed anything.

A good structure says: we need to understand whether this is primarily a revenue problem or a cost problem, and we need to understand whether it is specific to this airline or shared across the industry. Those two questions will tell us where to look. If it's a revenue problem unique to this airline, we look at their pricing, routes, and customer retention. If it's an industry-wide cost problem, we look at input costs that affect everyone. Get those two questions answered and the structure almost writes itself.

This is the move: instead of listing everything that could possibly be relevant, ask what the two or three questions that would most efficiently narrow the problem space are. That's your structure.

The practical execution matters too. Write it down — not in your head, on the paper in front of you. Speaking a structure before writing it almost always produces a messier structure than writing it first, because writing forces clarity that speaking lets you evade. When you do walk the interviewer through it, be explicit about the logic: "I want to start here because if the answer is X, it tells us Y and we can rule out Z." That kind of narrated reasoning is far more impressive than simply announcing "I'll look at revenues and costs."

One more thing about structure: it is allowed to evolve. If you learn something in the analysis phase that makes your initial structure look misguided, update the structure. Announce the update. "Based on what we just found, I think the more interesting question is actually over here — can I redirect the analysis?" Adapting your structure to new information is not a sign of weakness. It is a sign of thinking. Rigidly adhering to a structure that no longer fits the problem is the actual failure mode.

## Phase Three: Analysis as Hypothesis Testing

The analysis phase has a specific character that distinguishes good case analysis from competent-looking-but-useless analysis. Good analysis is hypothesis-driven. It proceeds by forming specific, testable propositions about what is causing the problem, and then looking for evidence that confirms or disconfirms them.

The alternative is data-gathering-driven analysis, which proceeds by collecting all available information and hoping a pattern emerges. This is not analysis. It is archaeology. And it takes too long, produces too much material to sort through, and often misses the actual answer because the actual answer is three levels below the surface and you never got there.

Here is what hypothesis-driven analysis looks like in practice. You've established that the airline's problem is a revenue problem, not a cost problem, and that it's specific to this airline rather than industry-wide. You form a hypothesis: the most likely explanation is that they've lost market share on high-value routes to a low-cost competitor. Now you test it. What does market share data show on the routes where they've seen the biggest revenue declines? Has a specific competitor entered those routes? What has happened to ticket prices on those routes? What has happened to load factors?

Each question is pointed. Each answer either supports the hypothesis, contradicts it, or — most usefully — refines it into a more specific version. "We're losing share on business routes because a competitor is undercutting us on price" is a different problem than "we're losing share because our reliability has declined and business travelers have switched." Both might fit the initial data, but they call for completely different responses. The hypothesis-testing approach gets you to that distinction efficiently.

The comparison habit belongs here too. Every number needs a reference point to be informative. A load factor of 79% means nothing by itself. A load factor of 79% against an industry average of 85% and a company benchmark of 83% two years ago means a great deal. When you are analyzing data, instinctively ask: is this number big or small? Compared to what baseline? In which direction has it moved? What rate of change is normal and what is alarming?

And through all of this: keep talking. Not continuously, not performatively, but enough that the interviewer can follow your reasoning and intervene if you're heading somewhere unproductive. Narrate the hypothesis you're testing and why. Flag when you're surprised by a number. Signal when a dead end has caused you to revise. The analysis phase is not a silent exam. It is a conversation in which you are demonstrating a process, not just producing a result.

## When You Get Stuck

You will get stuck. Every candidate gets stuck. What separates the candidates who recover from the ones who don't is almost entirely about how they handle the moment of being stuck.

The wrong response is to go quiet, stare at the paper, and hope the answer materializes. It rarely does, and the silence communicates anxiety rather than thought.

The right response is to be explicit about where you are and what you're trying to do. "I'm not immediately seeing what's driving the volume decline — let me think about what data would help me figure that out." This is not an admission of failure. It is a demonstration that you know you don't know something and you have a plan for figuring it out. That is actually a sophisticated response.

Interviewers frequently provide hints when candidates get stuck. The hints are often subtle — a pointed question, a specific number offered without being requested, an emphasis on a detail mentioned earlier. Stay alert to these. The interviewer is not cheating for you; they are giving you the nudge that a manager would give a junior analyst who needed it. Taking the hint and running with it is the right thing to do. Ignoring the hint and persisting in the wrong direction is not persistence — it is stubbornness.

There is also value in returning to your structure when you're stuck. The structure is a map. If you've lost your way, look at the map. Which parts have you covered? Which haven't you? Is there an area you set up to explore and then skipped? Very often, getting stuck in one branch of the analysis is a signal that you've already implicitly answered that branch and need to move to the next one. The structure tells you where that is.

## Phase Four: Recommendations That Actually Recommend Something

The recommendation is the deliverable. Everything before it was preparation. And yet, the recommendation is the phase where the most candidates produce the least.

The failure has a specific anatomy. The candidate has done solid analysis. They've identified the problem, traced it to a root cause, and have a reasonably clear sense of what could be done about it. Then, at the moment of synthesis, something happens. The candidate shifts from analyst mode into hedge mode. The recommendation becomes: "There are several options. Option A has advantages X and Y but disadvantages Z and W. Option B would address Z but creates new challenges around V. The company should consider its strategic objectives and risk tolerance in making this decision."

This is not a recommendation. This is a refusal to recommend, dressed up as rigor.

The reason candidates do this is understandable. Committing to a recommendation feels risky. What if it's wrong? What if the interviewer disagrees? What if there are factors you haven't considered? The hedged answer feels safer because it's technically defensible — you haven't said anything false.

But consulting is not a business that rewards technically defensible non-answers. Clients hire consultants because they need someone to actually take a position — to say "do this" with enough specificity and supporting logic that the client can act on it. The interviewer knows this. When they watch a candidate produce a hedged non-recommendation, they are not impressed by the candidate's careful acknowledgment of uncertainty. They are noting that the candidate cannot do the fundamental thing.

A good recommendation has three properties. It is specific: not "improve customer experience" but "reduce boarding time on domestic routes by implementing a new gate assignment system, targeting a 15% reduction in the next six months." It is prioritized: it identifies the most important action, not a comprehensive list of everything that might help. And it is grounded: it connects directly back to the analysis, so that the logic runs from problem to cause to response.

Apply the sanity check before you deliver it. Does the math hold up? Is the recommendation actually implementable given what you know about the client's resources? Does it address the right version of the problem? The sanity check is not about introducing doubt — it is about ensuring that you're not recommending something that falls apart on first examination.

Then deliver it clearly, without excessive hedging, with explicit acknowledgment of the key risk or limitation. "We recommend X because of Y and Z. The main risk is W, which we'd address by doing V." One sentence on risk is appropriate. Three paragraphs on everything that might go wrong is not a recommendation — it is anxiety in paragraph form.

## The Approach as Character, Not Technique

The four phases — understand, structure, analyze, recommend — are mechanical at the level of description. In practice, executing them well is not mainly a technical challenge. It is mainly a challenge of character.

The candidate who pauses genuinely at phase one, who asks for what they need and waits for the answer, is demonstrating patience. The candidate who builds a structure and says honestly when it needs updating is demonstrating intellectual honesty. The candidate who narrates their reasoning throughout is demonstrating that they think of analysis as communication, not just computation. The candidate who commits to a recommendation at the end, knowing it might be wrong, is demonstrating the kind of judgment under uncertainty that consultants need every day.

None of these are things you can fake convincingly for forty-five minutes. The interviewers have done this hundreds of times. They know what genuine intellectual engagement looks like versus performance of intellectual engagement. The preparation that actually helps is not the preparation that teaches you to perform confidence — it is the preparation that builds the real thing, by doing enough cases that the discipline becomes second nature and the anxiety has somewhere to go.

That is what the approach is actually for. Not to produce the right answer, but to produce the kind of thinking that tends to reach good answers, even in situations you haven't seen before. The interviewer is not hoping to hand you a familiar problem. They are hoping to watch you think your way through an unfamiliar one.

Show them that.

## A note about AI

The strategic approach is the move that separates a candidate who has practiced from one who has thought. The model has practiced. It has not thought, in the sense the interviewer means.

Where the model genuinely helps: stress-testing your strategic approach by playing devil's advocate against your framing. Models surface the obvious objections cleanly.

Where the model does damage: producing your strategic approach for you. Strategy in a case is a function of the case's specifics, and your read of those specifics is the read being graded.

The rule: model objects, you frame.
