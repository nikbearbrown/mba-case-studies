# Chapter 5 — Value Chain Analysis

Before we talk about value chains, let's talk about where money goes.

Pick any company that makes a physical product — say, a running shoe. Raw materials come in one end: rubber, foam, synthetic fabric, thread. A finished shoe comes out the other. Between those two points, something happens that is worth a great deal more than the sum of its inputs. The rubber and foam and fabric, assembled in the right configuration, by the right process, marketed to the right person through the right channel, becomes something a consumer will pay $150 for. The materials themselves cost perhaps $15. What accounts for the other $135?

That gap — between what inputs cost and what outputs sell for — is where value chain analysis lives. It is the analytical framework for understanding how companies actually create value, where they spend money creating it, and which parts of the process are working well versus which parts are leaking value they should be capturing.

This matters enormously in case interviews because a surprising fraction of all business problems, when traced carefully, turn out to be value chain problems in disguise. A company's margins are declining — where in the chain is value being lost? A competitor is undercutting on price — at which stage of production are they achieving cost advantages? A new market entry is failing — where in the chain does the company lack the capability to serve this market? These questions cannot be answered without a systematic way of decomposing how the business actually works.

---

## What a Value Chain Actually Is

Michael Porter introduced the formal concept of the value chain in 1985, but the underlying idea is older and simpler: a business is not a single thing, it is a sequence of activities, and each activity both consumes resources and creates (or destroys) value. The value chain is just a map of those activities, organized in the order they occur.

Porter divided activities into two categories. Primary activities are the ones directly involved in creating, delivering, and supporting the product: inbound logistics, operations, outbound logistics, marketing and sales, and service. Support activities are the ones that enable primary activities to function: procurement, technology development, human resource management, and firm infrastructure.

This taxonomy is useful for thinking comprehensively, but in case interviews, the version of value chain analysis you will use most is somewhat more granular and industry-specific than Porter's original. Rather than the generic five-primary-activity structure, you'll trace the specific sequence of activities through which this particular business creates value: how they source materials, how they make things, how they store and move things, how they sell things, and how they support things after the sale.

The version that appears in this book structures those activities as: Product Development and R&D → Sourcing of Materials → Manufacturing → Warehousing and Transportation Logistics → Distribution → Retail and Marketing → After-Sales Service and Support. This is a reasonable map for a physical goods company. For a software company, the activities look different — you'd emphasize product development and engineering heavily, logistics almost not at all, and customer success in place of after-sales repair. For a professional services firm, the chain transforms almost entirely into people-acquisition and knowledge-application stages.

The important principle is that the specific activities in a value chain are determined by the specific business, not by the framework. The framework is a prompt to think comprehensively, not a straitjacket that forces every business into the same shape.

## Why the Chain Metaphor Is Right

The word "chain" is doing real work here, and it's worth unpacking why.

A chain has a specific physical property: it is only as strong as its weakest link. Pull on a chain and it doesn't distribute force evenly — it snaps at the weakest point, regardless of how strong all the other links are. Value chains work similarly. A company can have world-class manufacturing but catastrophic distribution, and the catastrophic distribution will undermine the world-class manufacturing completely. The finished goods will sit in warehouses or arrive damaged or reach the wrong channels. Customers who might have been delighted by the product will never encounter it.

This property has a diagnostic implication. When you're analyzing a company's cost structure or profitability problems using a value chain, you're looking for the weak link. You're not trying to evaluate every stage comprehensively for its own sake — that would take forever and produce too much material to synthesize. You're trying to identify which stage is responsible for the problem, so you can concentrate analytical attention there.

How do you find the weak link? Two methods.

The first is to ask where costs are concentrated relative to what you'd expect. If a company is spending 40% of its COGS on logistics in an industry where that figure is typically 15%, you've found something worth examining. If manufacturing costs have been flat while distribution costs have risen 30% over two years, the chain is developing a weakness in a specific place.

The second is to ask where performance has deteriorated over time or diverged from competitors. If a company's on-time delivery rates have declined from 94% to 87% while a competitor's have held at 95%, that's a logistics problem. If customer satisfaction scores have dropped specifically on post-purchase support while the product itself receives high marks, that's an after-sales problem. The value chain gives you the vocabulary to name where the deterioration is happening.

The chain metaphor also captures something else important: activities are connected, not independent. What happens at one stage constrains and shapes what's possible at the next. Poor sourcing decisions create manufacturing problems downstream. Manufacturing defects create after-sales costs at the end. Over-investment in retail marketing that drives demand the supply chain can't fulfill creates customer experience failures everywhere. When you change something in one part of the chain, you need to trace the implications through to everything the changed activity connects to.

## Cost Structure as the Operational Lens

In case interviews, value chain analysis is most commonly applied as a cost analysis tool. The question it answers is: where does the money go, and is that distribution of spending appropriate?

This requires distinguishing between two types of costs that behave very differently: fixed costs and variable costs.

Fixed costs are the ones that don't change with volume — at least not in the short run. Factory rent is fixed whether you make 10,000 units or 100,000. The R&D team's salaries are fixed. The warehousing infrastructure has a fixed component. When you have high fixed costs, the unit economics of your business are highly sensitive to volume: as volume increases, fixed costs get spread over more units and profitability improves dramatically. As volume decreases, those costs concentrate on fewer units and margins compress just as dramatically.

Variable costs change with volume. Raw material costs rise when you make more units and fall when you make fewer. Packaging materials, shipping costs for individual orders, commissions to sales agents — these scale with the business. When variable costs dominate your cost structure, your margins per unit are more stable but your ability to achieve economies of scale is limited.

Understanding which costs in each stage of the value chain are fixed versus variable tells you how to think about the business's profitability dynamics. A company with high fixed costs in manufacturing benefits enormously from volume — every incremental unit sold above breakeven contributes almost entirely to profit. A company with high variable costs in distribution needs to negotiate better rates or find more efficient channels rather than simply selling more units.

The third distinction worth making is between direct and indirect costs. Direct costs are traceable to specific units of production: the leather in a shoe, the labor hours on a specific assembly line, the freight cost for a specific shipment. Indirect costs — overhead, management salaries, shared IT infrastructure — support the business generally but can't be cleanly attributed to any specific product or unit.

This matters for value chain analysis because direct costs tell you where the value creation is actually happening. If direct costs are rising in manufacturing while direct costs elsewhere are stable, the manufacturing process is becoming less efficient. Indirect costs are harder to trace but can be equally important: a company that has allowed overhead to balloon while direct costs stayed controlled has a different kind of problem, one that often requires structural rather than operational solutions.

## The Seven Stages in Detail

Let's walk through the chain stage by stage, because each stage has its own cost drivers and its own failure modes.

**Product Development and R&D** is where the value chain begins — often invisibly, because R&D costs are incurred long before any revenue appears. The relevant questions here are about investment level and effectiveness: is this company spending enough on R&D relative to competitors to maintain product relevance? Is the R&D pipeline generating products that customers actually want? Are intellectual property assets — patents, proprietary processes, trade secrets — being developed and protected in ways that create durable competitive advantage? The failure modes at this stage tend to be slow and hard to detect: inadequate R&D spending shows up as product obsolescence two or three years later, not immediately.

**Sourcing of Materials** is where costs first become concrete. The key questions are about procurement efficiency: what is the company paying for inputs relative to market prices? Do they have supplier concentration risk — too much dependence on a single supplier who can exert pricing power? Are they managing order quantities intelligently to minimize inventory costs while avoiding stockouts? Are there wastage or spoilage problems that are eating into the value of materials purchased? The best companies at this stage have long-term supplier relationships that create preferential pricing, volume commitments that enable planning, and alternative sourcing options that provide negotiating leverage.

**Manufacturing** is often the most complex stage to analyze because it has the most moving parts. Machinery costs, factory rent, electricity, labor hours, technology, capacity utilization, packaging, and defect rates all interact. The single most important concept here is capacity utilization: a factory running at 60% capacity has a very different unit economics profile than the same factory running at 90%, even if everything else is identical. Fixed costs are being divided over 50% more units in the second case. When a company's manufacturing margins are deteriorating, the first question is almost always whether volume has declined and the fixed cost burden has intensified as a result.

Defect rates deserve special attention because they have a multiplier effect. A defect caught in manufacturing costs whatever it costs to rework or scrap that unit. A defect that gets through manufacturing and into distribution costs more — you now also have logistics costs on a unit that generates no revenue. A defect that reaches the customer costs more still: you have all the upstream costs plus the after-sales service cost, plus the harder-to-quantify cost to brand equity and customer loyalty.

**Warehousing and Transportation Logistics** is the stage where physical goods companies most often have hidden cost problems. The costs here divide naturally into two: getting goods from manufacturing to warehouse (inbound logistics) and from warehouse to customer (outbound logistics). The relevant questions are about route optimization, vehicle utilization, storage costs, and the trade-off between inventory levels and service quality.

High inventory levels reduce the risk of stockouts but increase the cost of holding goods — tied-up capital, storage space, insurance, and the risk of obsolescence. Low inventory levels reduce holding costs but increase the risk of missing customer demand and the cost of expedited shipping when stockouts occur. Finding the right inventory level for each product in each market is genuinely hard and involves forecasting demand accurately, which most companies do imperfectly.

**Distribution** is where the company's go-to-market model becomes visible. Is the company selling direct to consumers, through retail partners, through wholesale distributors, through online channels, or some combination? Each channel has a different cost structure and a different margin profile. A company that sells through retail partners is sharing margin with those partners; a company that sells direct keeps more margin but bears the full customer acquisition cost. Channel mix decisions have profound effects on overall profitability and are often stickier than companies would like — channel partners develop expectations and contractual protections, and changing channel mix is slow and expensive.

**Retail and Marketing** is where demand gets created and converted into sales. The questions here are about marketing efficiency: what is the cost of acquiring a customer, how does that compare to the lifetime value of the customer, and are different channels generating customers at different cost-to-value ratios? Digital marketing has made these metrics measurable in ways that were impossible before — companies can now track the exact path from a digital advertisement to a purchase — but the proliferation of channels has also complicated the attribution question. Which channel deserves credit for a purchase that involved three touchpoints?

**After-Sales Service and Support** is the stage most frequently underweighted in strategic analysis, and it is often where significant value is either created or destroyed. The costs here include repairs, spare parts, returns processing, and the service level agreements that many business-to-business companies are contractually committed to. The failure mode is treating after-sales as a pure cost center rather than as a source of customer data, loyalty, and in some industries a substantial revenue stream. Companies that design products for serviceability — that make it easy and cheap to repair and maintain — have fundamentally different after-sales cost structures than companies that don't.

## How to Use Value Chain Analysis in a Case

In a case interview, value chain analysis is a diagnostic tool. You reach for it when the question is about cost structure, operational efficiency, or the feasibility of a strategic move that requires capability in specific parts of the chain.

The practical sequence is: identify the stage or stages where the problem is likely concentrated, form a hypothesis about what specific cost drivers within that stage are behaving abnormally, and then ask for the data that would confirm or disconfirm the hypothesis.

Here is an important discipline: do not audit the entire chain in sequence. Interviewers find this tedious, and it signals that you're using the framework as a crutch rather than as a tool. The chain is a search space. Your job is to identify the most promising part of the search space given what you know and go there directly, explaining why you're starting there.

"I'd like to focus on manufacturing and logistics first, because the margin compression over the past two years coincides with the opening of their new distribution center, and I'd expect the costs of ramping a new logistics infrastructure to show up most clearly in those two stages." That is a directed, hypothesis-driven use of the framework. "Let me start with R&D and work my way through all seven stages" is not.

The value chain is also essential for new market entry analysis, where the question is not just about external market attractiveness but about whether the company actually has the capabilities in each relevant stage to serve the new market. A company considering expansion into Southeast Asia needs to think about whether their existing sourcing relationships include suppliers who can serve that region cost-effectively, whether their manufacturing can accommodate the different product specifications that market might require, whether their logistics infrastructure extends there or would need to be built, and whether their distribution relationships transfer or need to be rebuilt from scratch. Missing capability in any stage can be a decisive barrier to entry.

## The Underlying Idea

The value chain works as an analytical tool because it encodes a specific insight about how businesses create value: value creation is not a single act but a sequence of acts, each of which adds something to the output the customer eventually pays for. The rubber becomes a shoe component; the shoe component becomes an assembled shoe; the assembled shoe becomes a packaged product; the packaged product becomes an available item in a retail channel; the available item becomes a purchase decision informed by marketing.

At each stage, value is either added — through efficient production, smart logistics, effective marketing — or destroyed — through defects, waste, channel friction, poor customer experience. The total value the company captures is the cumulative result of how well it manages each stage and the connections between them.

This is not a complicated idea. It is, in fact, a very simple idea. What makes it powerful is its systematicity — the discipline it imposes of looking at every stage, not just the glamorous ones. Companies that have excellent products but terrible distribution fail. Companies that have great logistics but no marketing capability fail. Companies that have superb customer acquisition but catastrophic after-sales support fail more slowly, but they fail.

The value chain is a reminder that excellence in one stage cannot compensate for failure in another. And in that sense it is one of the most honest frameworks in the entire toolkit — it does not let you get away with looking at the best parts of a business and averting your eyes from the rest.

## A note about AI

Value chain analysis is a structured decomposition. The model decomposes fluently. The risk is producing a decomposition that looks correct and treats every link as equally weighted.

Where the model genuinely helps: producing the canonical value chain for an unfamiliar industry quickly, so you have a starting map before you begin to challenge it. The map is generic on purpose.

Where the model does damage: deciding which links in the value chain matter most for this specific case. The weighting is the analysis; the decomposition is just the setup.

The rule: structure from the model; weighting from you.
